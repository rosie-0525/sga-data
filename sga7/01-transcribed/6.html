1. Formal existence theorem

We recall the notion of cofibered category (S.G.A.1.IV).

Let \(C\) be a fixed category. A cofibered category \(A\) over \(C\) is, by definition, a category \(A\) together with a functor \(P:A\to C\) such that

Ax.1. For any map \(f\) in \(C\) and any object \(a\) in \(A\) with \(P(a)\) = the source of \(f\), there exists an \(f\)-morphism \(\alpha:a\to b\) which is cocartesian (i.e. for any \(f\)-morphism \(\alpha':a\to b'\) there exists a unique \(T\)-morphism \(\tau:b\to b'\) in \(A\) such that \(\alpha'=\tau\alpha\) where \(T\) = the target of \(f\)).

Ax.2. A composit of cocartesian morphism in \(A\) is again cocartesian.

The following properties of a cofibered category \(P:A\to C\) follow immediately from the definitions:

(cancellation) Let \(a\mathrel{\substack{\alpha\\[-.5ex]\rightrightarrows\\[-.5ex]\beta}}a'\) be any two cocartesian maps such that \(P(\alpha)=P(\beta)\). If there exists a cocartesian map \(b\xrightarrow{\gamma}a\) such that \(\alpha\gamma=\beta\gamma\), then \(\alpha=\beta\).

(factorization) Given cocartesian maps \(a\xrightarrow{\alpha}a'\), \(a\xrightarrow{\beta}a''\), there exists a cocartesian map \(a'\xrightarrow{\gamma}a''\) such that \(\gamma\alpha=\beta\) if and only if there exists \(f:P(a')\to P(a'')\) in \(C\) such that \(fP(\alpha)=P(\beta)\). Furthermore \(\gamma\) is unique.

Let \(A\) be a cofibered category over \(C\). For each object \(S\) in \(C\) we denote by \(A(S)\) the subcategory of \(A\) whose objects are the objects \(a\) in \(A\) such that \(P(a)=S\), and \[\operatorname{Hom}_{A(S)}(a,a')=\{\alpha\in\operatorname{Hom}_A(a,a')\mid P(\alpha)=\operatorname{id}_S\}.\] A cofibered groupoid over \(C\) is a cofibered category \(A\) over \(C\) such that \(A(S)\) is a groupoid for every object \(S\) in \(C\). We recall that a groupoid is a category in which every morphism is an isomorphism. Thus, a cofibered groupoid over \(C\) is a cofibered category \(P:A\to C\) such that every map in \(A\) is cocartesian. Furthermore, for every map \(\alpha\in\operatorname{Fl}(A)\), \(\alpha\) is an isomorphism in \(A\) if and only if \(P(\alpha)\) is an isomorphism.

Throughout the rest of this section, we fix a complete noetherian local ring \(\Lambda\) with the residue field \(k\), and we set \(C_\Lambda\) to be the category of artinian local \(\Lambda\)-algebras with the same residue field \(k\), where the maps are local homomorphisms over \(\Lambda\). From 1.2 on, a cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) is always assumed to have the property that \(A(k)=\{e\}\), where \(\{e\}\) denotes the category in which \(\operatorname{Hom}_{\{e\}}(a,b)\) consists of one and only one element for any two objects \(a,b\) in \(\{e\}\). A map \(a'\xrightarrow{\alpha}a\) in \(A\) will be called simply a \(C_\Lambda\)-map if \(p(a')=p(a)\) and \(p(\alpha)=1_{p(a)}\). A map \(a'\xrightarrow{\alpha}a\) on \(A\) will be called surjective, by abuse of language, if \(p(\alpha):p(a')\to p(a)\) is surjective in \(C_\Lambda\).

Ex.1.1.(a) Let \(F:C\to(\text{categories})\) be a functor. Define the category \(\underline F\) as follows: an object in \(\underline F\) is a pair \((S,a)\) where \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C)\) and \(a\in\operatorname{ob}F(S)\), and a map \((S,a)\to(S',a')\) is a pair \((f,u')\) where \(f:S\to S'\) is a map in \(C\) and \(u':F(f)a\to a'\) is a map in \(F(S')\). Composition of two maps \[(S,a)\xrightarrow{(f,u')}(S',a')\xrightarrow{(g,u'')}(S'',a'')\] is given by \((gf,u''\cdot(F(g)u')):(S,a)\to(S'',a'')\). With respect to the functor \(P:\underline F\to C\) given by \((S,a)\mapsto S\), \(\underline F\) becomes a cofibered category over \(C\). If \(F(S)\) is a groupoid for every \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C)\), then \(\underline F\) is a cofibered groupoid over \(C\). In particular, a functor \(F:C\to(\text{groups})\) yields a cofibered group, and a functor \(F:C\to(\mathrm{Ens})\) yields a cofibered discrete groupoid. We also note that a natural transformation \(\varphi:F\to G\), where \(F,G:C\to(\text{categories})\) are functors, induces a cocartesian functor \(\varphi:\underline F\to\underline G\) over \(C\).

Ex.1.1.(b) Let \(X,Y\) be schemes over \(\Lambda\) together with a fixed isomorphism \(X\otimes_\Lambda k\xrightarrow{\tau}Y\otimes_\Lambda k\). Then the functor \(\operatorname{Isom}_{X,Y}:C_\Lambda\to(\text{groupoids})\) given by \(\operatorname{Isom}_{X,Y}(S)\) = the set of isomorphisms \(X\otimes_\Lambda S\xrightarrow{\sigma}Y\otimes_\Lambda S\) such that \(\sigma\otimes_\Lambda k=\tau\) yields a cofibered groupoid \(P:\underline{\operatorname{Isom}}_{X,Y}\to C_\Lambda\).

Ex.1.1.(c) Let \(X_0\) be a fixed scheme over \(k\), and let \(M_{X_0}\) be the category consisting of deformations of \(X_0\) over \(C_\Lambda\) (see §4). Thus an object in \(M_{X_0}\) is a pair \((R,X)\) where \(R\) is in \(C_\Lambda\) and \(X\) is a deformation of \(X_0\) to \(R\). Then the functor \(P:M_{X_0}\to C_\Lambda\) given by \((R,X)\mapsto R\) defines a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\).

Ex.1.1.(d) For any groupoid \(X\), we denote by \([X]\) the discrete groupoid consisting of the isomorphism classes of objects in \(X\). If \(P:F\to C_\Lambda\) is a cofibered groupoid, we obtain a functor \([F]:C_\Lambda\to(\mathrm{Ens})\) given by \([F](S)=[F(S)]\), and in turn a cofibered discrete groupoid, which is, by abuse of notation, denoted by \([F]\).

Definition 1.2.

A cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) is called semi-homogeneous if the following properties hold:

  1. (1) every diagram \[\xymatrix{a''\ar@{.>}[r]\ar@{.>}[d]&a'\ar[d]\\a_1\ar[r]&a}\] of solid arrows in \(A\) where \(a'\to a\) is surjective can be completed into a commutative diagram including dotted arrows.
  2. (2) Let \(R\times_k k[\varepsilon]\mathrel{\substack{\pi_1\\[-.5ex]\rightrightarrows\\[-.5ex]\pi_2}}R, k[\varepsilon]\) be the projection maps, where \(k[\varepsilon]\) is the ring of dual numbers over \(k\), and let \(a'\mathrel{\substack{\alpha\\[-.5ex]\rightrightarrows\\[-.5ex]\beta}}a\), \(b'\) be \(\pi_1\)-maps in \(A\). Then there exists a \(C_\Lambda\)-map \(\gamma:a'\to b'\) with \(\alpha=\beta\gamma\) if and only if \((\pi_2)_*(a')\approx(\pi_2)_*(b')\).

Remark 1.3.(a) A reformulation of the notion in terms of 2-categories will be given later in 2.6(b). We note that if \(A\) is semi-homogeneous, then so is \([A]\), and in particular 1.2(2) entails the canonical map \[[A(R\times_k k[\varepsilon])]\longrightarrow[A(R)]\times[A(k[\varepsilon])]\] to be bijective.

Remark 1.3.(b) Consider a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&a'\ar[dl]_{v_1}\ar[dr]^{v_2}&\\a_1\ar[dr]_{u_1}&&a_2\ar[dl]^{u_2}\\&a&}\] in a cofibered category \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\). Set \(R=P(a)\), \(R'=P(a')\) and \(R_i=P(a_i)\) for \(i=1,2\). If we set \(a''\) to be the “direct image” under the map \((P(v_1),P(v_2)):R'\to R_1\times_RR_2\), we obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&a'\ar[ddl]_{v_1}\ar[ddr]^{v_2}\ar[d]&\\&a''\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&\\a_1\ar[dr]_{u_1}&&a_2\ar[dl]^{u_2}\\&a&}\] Thus 1.2(1) can be replaced by

(1)′: every diagram \(\xymatrix@C=2em{a_1\ar[dr]&&a_2\ar[dl]\\&a&}\) in \(A\) above \(\xymatrix@C=2em{R_1\ar[dr]&&R_2\ar[dl]\\&R&}\) in \(C_\Lambda\), where \(R_2\to R\) is surjective can be completed to a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=3em{&a'\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&&&R_1\times_RR_2\ar[dl]_{\pi_1}\ar[dr]^{\pi_2}\\a_1\ar[dr]&&a_2\ar[dl]&R_1\ar[dr]&&R_2\ar[dl]\\&a&&&R&}\] where \(\pi_i\) (\(i=1,2\)) are projections.

Remark 1.3.(c) If \(A\) is a semi-homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), then \([A(k[\varepsilon])]\) is canonically provided with a vector space structure over \(k\), where \(k[\varepsilon]\) is the ring of dual numbers over \(k\). Indeed, let \(V\) be the category of finite-dimensional vector spaces over \(k\). Then any functor \(H:V\to(\mathrm{Ens})\) preserving the product is canonically factored through \[\xymatrix{V\ar[rr]^H\ar[dr]_{\widetilde H}&&(\mathrm{Ens})\\&\underline V\ar[ur]&}\] where \(\underline V\to(\mathrm{Ens})\) is the forgetful functor. On the other hand, the functor \(G:V\to C_\Lambda\) given by \(W\mapsto k[W]=k\oplus W\), with \(W^2=0\), transforms products in \(V\) into fibre products over \(k\) in \(C_\Lambda\). If \(A\) is a semi-homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), then \[[A(k[V]\times_k k[W])]\longrightarrow[A(k[V])]\times[A(k[W])]\] is bijective by 1.3(a), and the functor \(V\mapsto[A(k[V])]\) commutes with products, hence the result.

Definition 1.4.

(1) Let \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)\). A small extension of \(R\) in \(C_\Lambda\) is a surjective map \(R'\xrightarrow{f}R\) in \(C_\Lambda\) such that \((\ker f)^2=0\) and \(\ker f\simeq k\). In other words, \(R'\xrightarrow{f}R\) in \(C_\Lambda\) is a small extension of \(R\) if \[0\longrightarrow k\xrightarrow{t}R'\xrightarrow{f}R\longrightarrow0\] is exact for some \(t\in R'\) with \(t^2=0\).

(2) Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), and let \(a\) be an object in \(A\). A small prolongation of \(a\) is an arrow \(a'\xrightarrow\alpha a\) in \(A\) such that \(P(\alpha)\) is a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\). An arrow \(a'\xrightarrow\alpha a\) in \(A\) will be called “versal for small prolongations of \(a\)” if, for every small prolongation \(a'_1\to a\) in \(A\), there exists a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{a'\ar[rr]\ar[dr]_{\alpha}&&a'_1\ar[dl]\\&a&}\]

Proposition 1.5.

Let \(A\) be a semi-homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\).

(1) Let \(R\times_k k[\varepsilon]\mathrel{\substack{\pi_1\\[-.5ex]\rightrightarrows\\[-.5ex]\pi_2}}R,k[\varepsilon]\) be the projection maps in \(C_\Lambda\), and let \(a'\xrightarrow\alpha a\) be a \(\pi_1\)-map. Then there exists a map \(a\xrightarrow\beta a'\) such that \(\alpha\beta=1_a\) if and only if there exists a map \(a\longrightarrow(\pi_2)_*a'\).

(2) Let \(R'\xrightarrow fR\) be a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\), and consider a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em{&a''\ar[dl]_{\alpha'}\ar[dr]^{\alpha'_1}&\\a'\ar[dr]_{\alpha}&&a'_1\ar[dl]^{\alpha_1}\\&a&} \quad\text{above}\quad\xymatrix@C=4em{&R'\times_RR'\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&\\R'\ar[dr]&&R'\ar[dl]\\&R&}\] Define the canonical map \(\widetilde f:R'\times_RR'\longrightarrow k[\ker f]\) by \(\widetilde f(r'_1,r'_2)=r'_1(0)+(r'_1-r'_2)\). If there exists a map \(a'\longrightarrow(\widetilde f)_*(a'')\), then there exists a map \(\gamma:a'\longrightarrow a\) such that \(\alpha=\alpha_1\gamma\).

Proof. (1) The part “only if” is clear. Now assume that there exists a map \(\varphi:a\longrightarrow(\pi_2)_*a'\). If we set \(h:R\to R\times_k k[\varepsilon]\) by \(h=1\times P(\varphi)\), then the composite map \(R\xrightarrow hR\times_k k[\varepsilon]\xrightarrow{\pi_1}R\) is the identity, and therefore we get maps \(a\mathrel{\substack{\gamma\\[-.5ex]\rightleftarrows\\[-.5ex]\alpha_1}}h_*a\) such that \(\alpha_1\gamma=1_a\), where \(P(\gamma)=h\) and \(P(\alpha_1)=\pi_1\). On the other hand, \(\pi_2h=P(\varphi)\) and hence there exists a \(\pi_2\)-map \(h_*a\to(\pi_2)_*a'\). This means that \((\pi_2)_*(h_*a)=(\pi_2)_*a'\), and therefore by 1.2(2) we have a \(C_\Lambda\)-map \(u:h_*a\to a'\) such that \(\alpha u=\alpha_1\). Define \(\beta:a\to a'\) by \(\beta=u\gamma\). Then \(\alpha\beta=\alpha u\gamma=\alpha_1\gamma=1_a\).

(2) The map \(1\times\widetilde f:R'\times_RR'\to R'\times_k k[\ker f]\) is an isomorphism compatible with the projections on the first factor, and \(\pi_2(1\times\widetilde f)=\widetilde f\), where \(\pi_2:R'\times_k k[\ker f]\to k[\ker f]\) is the projection map. We note that \(k[\ker f]\simeq k[\varepsilon]\) since \(R'\xrightarrow fR\) is a small extension. Therefore, if there exists a map \(a'\to(\widetilde f)_*a''=(\pi_2)_*[(1\times\widetilde f)_*a'']\), then by (1) above there exists a map \(\gamma':a'\to a''\) such that \(\alpha'_1\gamma'=1_{a'_1}\). Define \(\gamma:a'\to a\) by \(\gamma=\alpha'\gamma'\). We then have \(\alpha_1\gamma=\alpha\).

Corollary 1.6.

Let \(A\) be a semi-homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\) and consider a diagram \[\xymatrix{a'\ar[dr]_{\alpha}&&a_1\ar[dl]^{\alpha_1}\\&a&}\] in \(A\) where \(\alpha_1\) is a small prolongation. Assume that \(a\) is versal for small prolongations of \(e\). Then there exists a map \(a'\xrightarrow\rho a_1\) such that \(\alpha=\alpha_1\rho\) in \(A\) if and only if there exists a map \(f:P(a')\to P(a_1)\) such that \(P(\alpha)=P(\alpha_1)f\) in \(C_\Lambda\).

Proof: Assume that \(P(\alpha)=P(\alpha_1)f\) where \(f:P(a')\to P(a_1)\). Replacing \(a'\) by its direct image under \(f\), we may and shall assume that \(P(\alpha)=P(\alpha_1)\). Set \(R'=P(a_1)\), \(R=P(a)\). Since \(A\) is semi-homogeneous, we get a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em{&a''\ar[dl]_\beta\ar[dr]^{\beta_1}&\\a'\ar[dr]_\alpha&&a_1\ar[dl]^{\alpha_1}\\&a&} \quad\text{above}\quad\xymatrix@C=4em{&R'\times_RR'\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&\\R'\ar[dr]&&R'\ar[dl]\\&R&}\] Since \(a\) is versal for small prolongations of \(e\), so is \(a'\) and therefore our statement follows from 1.5(2).

Definition 1.7.

For each object \(R\) in \(C_\Lambda\) we put \(\overline\Omega_R=M/(m_\Lambda R+M^2)\), where \(m_\Lambda,M\) are the maximal ideals of \(\Lambda,R\) respectively. It is a finite-dimensional vector space over \(k\), and \(R\mapsto\overline\Omega_R\) defines a functor \(\overline\Omega:C_\Lambda\to V\), where \(V\) is the category of finite-dimensional vector spaces over \(k\). If \(A\) is a cofibered category over \(C_\Lambda\), then the composite functor \(A\xrightarrow P C_\Lambda\to V\) is, by abuse of notation denoted by \(\overline\Omega\) again.

Definition 1.9.

Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). An object \(\xi\) in \(\widehat A\) is called a quasi-initial object of \(\widehat A\) if for every object \(a\) in \(\widehat A\) there exists a map \(\xi\to a\) in \(\widehat A\). A quasi-initial object \(\xi\) of \(\widehat A\) is called minimal if the morphism \(\xi:h_R\to[\widehat A]\) induces a bijection \[\xi(k[\varepsilon]):\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,k[\varepsilon])\to[A(k[\varepsilon])],\] where \(R=P(\xi)\). An object \(\xi\) in \(\widehat A\) is called a versal formal object of \(A\) if every surjective map \(\alpha:a'\to a\) in \(\widehat A\) induces a surjective map \(\operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat A}(\xi,a')\to\operatorname{Hom}_{\widehat A}(\xi,a)\). A versal formal object \(\xi\) of \(A\) is called minimal if the morphism \(\xi:h_R\to[\widehat A]\) induces a bijection \(\xi(k[\varepsilon]):\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,k[\varepsilon])\to[A(k[\varepsilon])]\), where \(R=P(\xi)\).

(ii) Let \(\xi\xrightarrow\alpha\xi\) be a map in \(\widehat A\). Theorem 1.11. (Schlessinger)

Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). Then \(A\) admits a minimally-versal formal object if and only if

  1. (i) \(A\) is semi-homogeneous
  2. (ii) \([A(k[\varepsilon])]\) is a finite-dimensional vector space over \(k\) (cf. 1.3(c)).

Proof: (Necessity) Let \(\xi\) in \(\widehat A\) be a minimally-versal formal object of \(A\), and consider a diagram \[\xymatrix{a_1\ar[dr]&a_2\ar[dl]^\alpha\\&a} \] in \(A\) where \(\alpha\) is surjective. Since \(\xi\) is a quasi-initial object of \(\widehat A\), there exists a map \(\xi\to a_1\). Since \(\alpha\) is surjective, the composit map \(\xi\to a_1\to a\) can be lifted to \(a_2\) i.e., we obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&\xi\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&\\a_1\ar[dr]&&a_2\ar[dl]^\alpha\\&a&}\] which proves the condition 1.2(1). We now prove the condition 1.2(2). Let \[\xymatrix{S\mathbin{\times_k}k[\varepsilon]\ar@<.5ex>[r]^{\pi_1}\ar@<-.5ex>[r]_{\pi_2}&S\\ &k[\varepsilon]}\] be the projections, and let \[\xymatrix{a'\ar[dr]_\alpha&b'\ar[dl]^\beta\\&a}\] be \(\pi_1\)-maps in \(A\). By the definition of \(\xi\), we obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&\xi\ar[dl]_u\ar[dr]^v&\\a'\ar[dr]_\alpha&&b'\ar[dl]^\beta\\&a&}\] and in particular we have \(\pi_1P(u)=\pi_1P(v)\). Assume now that \((\pi_2)_*a'\simeq(\pi_2)_*b'\). We then have \(\pi_2P(u)=\pi_2P(v)\) because of the minimality of \(\xi\), and hence \[P(u)=\pi_1P(u)\mathbin{\times}\pi_2P(u)=\pi_1P(v)\mathbin{\times}\pi_2P(v)=P(v).\] Since \(A\) is a cofibered groupoid, we obtain a unique isomorphism \(a'\xrightarrow\rho b'\) in \(A(S\mathbin{\times_k}k[\varepsilon])\) such that \(\rho u=v\). Then \(\beta\rho u=\alpha u\) entails that \(\beta\rho=\alpha\) since \(A\) is a cofibered category.

(Sufficiency) Let \(c_1,c_2,\ldots,c_n\) be objects in \(A(k[\varepsilon])\) which form a \(k\)-basis of the vector space \([A(k[\varepsilon])]\), and choose \(a_1\) in \(A(k[\varepsilon]\mathbin{\times_k}\cdots\mathbin{\times_k}k[\varepsilon])\) such that \((\pi_j)_*a_1\simeq c_j\). Then \[\widetilde a_1(k[\varepsilon]):\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R_1,k[\varepsilon]) \longrightarrow[A(k[\varepsilon])]\] is bijective, where \(R_1=P(a_1)\). In particular \(a_1\to e\) is versal for small prolongations of \(e\). Choose \(a_2\to a_1\) which is versal for small prolongations of \(a_1\) such that \(\overline\Omega_{a_2}\to\overline\Omega_{a_1}\) is bijective. We then choose \(a_3\to a_2\), versal for small prolongations of \(a_2\) such that \(\overline\Omega_{a_3}\to\overline\Omega_{a_2}\) is bijective etc., and set \(\xi=\varprojlim_n a_n\). We claim that \(\xi\) is a minimally-versal formal object of \(A\). Indeed, since \(\overline\Omega_{a_n}\to\overline\Omega_{a_{n-1}}\) is bijective for all \(n\), \(\overline\Omega_\xi\to\overline\Omega_{a_1}\) is bijective so that \[\xi(k[\varepsilon]):\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,k[\varepsilon]) \longrightarrow[A(k[\varepsilon])]\] is bijective. Now consider a diagram \[\xymatrix{&a'\ar[d]^\alpha\\\xi\ar[r]^h&a}\] where \(\alpha\) is surjective in \(A\). We must show that \(h\) can be lifted to \(a'\). For this, one may assume, by induction, that \(a'\) is a small prolongation of \(a\). Since \(P(a)\) is an artinian ring, \(h:\xi\to a\) is factored as a composit map \(\xi\to a_q\to a\) for some \(q\). Since \(A\) is semi-homogeneous, we get a diagram \[\xymatrix{a''\ar[r]\ar[d]^{\alpha'}&a'\ar[d]^\alpha\\\xi\ar[r]&a_q\ar[r]&a}\\] where \(\alpha'\) is also a small prolongation. We then obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&&a''\ar[d]^{\alpha'}\\\xi\ar[r]&a_{q+1}\ar[r]&a_q}\] and therefore we are done.

Remark 1.12. Let \[\cdots\longrightarrow a_n\longrightarrow a_{n-1}\longrightarrow\cdots \longrightarrow a_2\longrightarrow a_1\longrightarrow a_0=e\] be an infinite sequences of arrows in \(A\) such that \[\left\{\begin{array}{l} a_i\text{ is versal for small prolongations of }a_{i-1},\\ P(a_i)\longrightarrow P(a_{i-1})\text{ is surjective},\\ \overline\Omega_{a_i}\longrightarrow\overline\Omega_{a_{i-1}}\text{ is bijective for }i\gg0. \end{array}\right.\] Then the above proof shows that \(\varprojlim a_i\) is a versal formal object of \(A\).

Remark 1.13. Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). If \(A\) has the property 1.11(i) and (ii), then so does \([A]\), and \(\xi\in\operatorname{ob}(\widehat A)\) is a minimally-versal formal object of \(A\) if and only if it is so for \([A]\) by 1.10(ii).

Remark 1.14. Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), admitting a minimally-versal formal object \(\xi\). Then an invariant of \(R=P(\xi)\), which is a complete local \(\Lambda\)-algebra of formally finite type over \(\Lambda\), is automatically an invariant of \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\). Therefore, for example, we shall say that \(A\) is (formally) smooth over \(C_\Lambda\) if \(R\) is (formally) smooth over \(\Lambda\) i.e., a formal power-series algebra over \(\Lambda\), or we say that \(A\) is irreducible over \(C_\Lambda\) if \(\operatorname{Spec}(R)\) is irreducible etc. In the next section we introduce the notion of “smoothness” of a functor \(A\to B\) over \(C_\Lambda\), where \(A,B\) are cofibered categories in groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). It turns out (cf. 2.4) that “\(A\) is (formally) smooth over \(C_\Lambda\)” is equivalent to “\(P:A\to C_\Lambda\) is a smooth functor”, and therefore no confusion arises. We also define the dimension of \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) by \(\dim_{C_\Lambda}A=\dim_\Lambda k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R\).

For this we note the following fact: Let \(C_k\) be the category of artinian local \(k\)-algebras with the same residue field \(k\). Then \(C_k\to C_\Lambda\) is a fully-faithful imbedding, whose (left) adjoint functor \(C_\Lambda\to C_k\) is given by \(R\mapsto k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R\). Let \(A|C_k\) be the pull-back of \(P:A\to C_\Lambda\) under \(C_k\to C_\Lambda\). Then it is trivial to see that if \(\xi\) is a minimally-versal formal object for \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\), then \(k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\xi\) is a minimally-versal formal object for \(A|C_k\) over \(C_k\), where \(\xi\to k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\xi\) is a cocartesian arrow above \(R\to k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R\). It follows that \(\dim_{C_\Lambda}A=\dim_{C_k}(A|C_k)\).

Let \(\Lambda\) and \(K\) be complete noetherian local rings, with \(K\) a \(\Lambda\)-algebra. We assume that

The most interesting case is when \(K=K'\) and \(K\) is a finite inseparable extension of \(k\).

We denote by \(\widehat C_{\Lambda,K}\) the category whose objects are the complete noetherian local rings \(R\), provided with a structure of \(\Lambda\)-algebra and with a \(\Lambda\)-epimorphism \(s:R\longrightarrow K\). If \(K\) is artinian, so is any object in \(C_{\Lambda,K}\).

The role played before by \(k[\varepsilon]\) will be played here by \(D_{K'}(K)\).

As explained in 1.8, any cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_{\Lambda,K}\) extends in a natural way to a cofibered category \(\widehat A\) over \(\widehat C_{\Lambda,K}\). A map in \(A\) or \(\widehat A\) will be called surjective if the corresponding homomorphism of algebras in \(C_{\Lambda,K}\) or in \(\widehat C_{\Lambda,K}\) is.

We assume that \(A(K)=\{e\}\).

Definition 1.16.

A cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_{\Lambda,K}\) is called semi-homogeneous if the following properties hold:

2. Prorepresentable cofibered groupoids.

Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), admitting a minimally-versal formal object \(\xi\). In general we can not recover \(A\) (up to \(C_\Lambda\)-equivalence) out of \(\xi\) alone. One has to know when two maps \(R\mathrel{\substack{f\\[-.5ex]\rightrightarrows\\[-.5ex]g}}S\) yield a \(C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism \(\xi(f)\longrightarrow\xi(g)\), where \(R=P(\xi)\). As an application of 1.11 and 1.12 we now consider the question of “recovering \(A\)” up to \(C_\Lambda\)-equivalence. Indeed, we shall have a quite satisfactory answer applicable to a wide class of cofibered groupoids.

A number of cofibered groupoids which usually occur in practice enjoy a slightly stronger property than those of semi-homogeneous ones, as it will be defined explicitly in 2.5. To investigate them it is convenient to rephrase what we have done so far in terms of “smooth functors”. All the notations and terminologies in the previous section remain in force throughout in this section.

Definition 2.1.

Let \(\varphi:A\longrightarrow B\) be a functor of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). We say that \(\varphi\) is smooth if every surjective map \(\beta:b'\longrightarrow\varphi(a)\) in \(B\) can be completed to a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{ \varphi(a')\ar[r]&b'\ar[d]^\beta\\ &\varphi(a)\ar@{<-}[ul]_{\varphi(\alpha)} }\tag{*}\] for some \(\alpha:a'\longrightarrow a\) in \(A\). We say that \(\varphi\) is minimally smooth if it is smooth and \[\varphi(k[\varepsilon]):[A(k[\varepsilon])]\longrightarrow[B(k[\varepsilon])]\] is bijective.

Remark 2.2(a) Let \(\varphi:A\longrightarrow B\) be a functor of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). We note that “\(\varphi\) is smooth” \(\Longleftrightarrow\) “every surjective map \(\beta:b'\longrightarrow\varphi(a)\) in \(B\) can be completed into a commutative diagram (*) such that \(\varphi(a')\longrightarrow b'\) is a \(C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism” \(\Longleftrightarrow\) “every small prolongation \(\beta:b'\longrightarrow\varphi(a)\) in \(B\) can be completed into a commutative diagram (*)”.

Remark 2.2(b) For any cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\), the natural functor \[A\longrightarrow[A]\] is minimally smooth.

Remark 2.2(c) For each object \(R\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\), we set \(h_R:C_\Lambda\longrightarrow(\mathrm{Ens})\) to be the functor defined by \[h_R(S)=\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,S)\quad\text{for all }S\text{ in }C_\Lambda.\] It associates a cofibered discrete groupoid \(\underline h_R\) over \(C_\Lambda\), and a map \(R\longrightarrow S\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) induces a functor \(\underline h_S\longrightarrow\underline h_R\) of cofibered groupoids. The functor \(\underline h_S\longrightarrow\underline h_R\) induced by a map \(R\longrightarrow S\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) is smooth if and only if \(S\) is a formal power-series ring over \(R\).

Remark 2.2(d) Given an object \(R\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\), let us denote by \((R,C_\Lambda)\) the category of arrows in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) with the source \(R\) and a target in \(C_\Lambda\). Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). Given \(\xi\in\operatorname{ob}\widehat A\) with \(P(\xi)=R\), let \(A'\longrightarrow(R,C_\Lambda)\) be the pull-back of \(A\longrightarrow C_\Lambda\) under the canonical functor \((R,C_\Lambda)\longrightarrow C_\Lambda\) and choose a section \(\xi^\#\) such that \(\xi^\#(1_R)=\xi\). (This simply means that we choose, for each map \(R\xrightarrow f S\), a \(f\)-map \(\xi\longrightarrow\xi(f)\)). We then obtain a functor \(\xi^\#:\underline h_R\longrightarrow A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) by setting \((R\xrightarrow f S)\longmapsto\xi(f)\). Of course this functor does not depend on the choice of \(\xi^\#\) up to natural equivalence of functors. Now “\(\xi^\#:\underline h_R\longrightarrow A\) is (minimally) smooth” means that every surjective map \(a'\xrightarrow\alpha\xi(f)\) in \(A\) can be completed into a commutative diagram \[ \xymatrix{ \xi(f')\ar[r]\ar[dr]&a'\ar[d]^\alpha\\ &\xi(f) } \] (and \(\underline h_R(k[\varepsilon])\longrightarrow A(k[\varepsilon])\) is bijective), which means precisely that \(\xi\) is a (minimally) versal formal object of \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\). Thus we conclude that the existence of a (minimally) versal formal object of \(A\) is equivalent to the existence of a (minimally) smooth functor \(\underline h_R\longrightarrow A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) for some \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(\widehat C_\Lambda)\).

The following general properties on smoothness are trivial to verify.

Proposition 2.3.

Let \(A\xrightarrow\varphi B\xrightarrow\psi C\) be functors of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\).

  1. (a) If \(\varphi,\psi\) are both smooth, then so is \(\psi\circ\varphi\).
  2. (b) If \(\psi\circ\varphi\) is smooth and \(\varphi\) is surjective on isomorphism classes of objects, then \(\psi\) is smooth.

Remark 2.4. Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), admitting a versal formal object \(\xi\). This means, by 2.2(d), that \(\xi^\#:\underline h_R\longrightarrow A\) is smooth, where \(R=P(\xi)\). Therefore we have, “\(A\) is (formally) smooth over \(C_\Lambda\) (c.f. 1.13) i.e., \(R\) is a formal power-series algebra over \(\Lambda\)” \(\Longleftrightarrow\) “the composite functor \(\underline h_R\xrightarrow{\xi^\#}A\xrightarrow P C_\Lambda\;(=h_\Lambda)\) is smooth” \(\Longleftrightarrow\) “\(A\xrightarrow P C_\Lambda\) is a smooth functor” by 2.3(b).

We now consider a stronger property than being semi-homogeneous.

Definition 2.5.

Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). It is called homogeneous if every diagram \[ \xymatrix{&a'\ar[d]\\b'\ar[r]&a} \] where \(a'\longrightarrow a\) is surjective admits a fibre-product \(a'\times_a b'\) in \(A\) such that \[P(a'\times_a b')=P(a')\mathop{\times}_{P(a)}P(b').\]

Remark 2.6(a) Let \(A\) be a cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), and consider a diagram \[ \xymatrix{&a'\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&&R_1\times_RR_2\ar[dl]_{\pi_1}\ar[dr]^{\pi_2}\\ a_1\ar[dr]&&a_2\ar[dl]&R_1\ar[dr]&&R_2\ar[dl]\\&a&&&R} \tag{*} \] in \(C_\Lambda\), where \(\pi_i\) are the projections. If a fibre-product \(a_1\times_a a_2\) exists in \(A\), we obtain a unique map \(\varphi:a'\longrightarrow a_1\times_a a_2\) compatible with the projections. If \(P(a_1\times_a a_2)=R_1\times_RR_2\), then \(P(\varphi)\) is the identity map on \(R_1\times_RR_2\) and therefore \(\varphi\) is a \(C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism i.e., \(a'=a_1\times_a a_2\). In other words, if \(A\) is a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), every diagram (*) defines a fibre-product \(a_1\times_a a_2\) provided \(R_1\longrightarrow R\) is surjective in \(C_\Lambda\). This fact entails immediately that a homogeneous cofibered groupoid is, a fortiori, semi-homogeneous.

Remark 2.6(b) Consider a diagram \[\xymatrix{X\ar[dr]_\varphi&&Y\ar[dl]^\psi\\&Z} \] of categories and functors. The ordinary fibre-product \(X\times_ZY\) is a category in which an object is a pair \((x,y)\) such that \(\varphi(x)=\psi(y)\), and a map \((x,y)\longrightarrow(x',y')\) consists of a pair \(\alpha:x\longrightarrow x'\), \(\beta:y\longrightarrow y'\) such that \(\varphi(\alpha)=\psi(\beta)\). The fibre-product \(X\mathop{\times}\limits_Z^2Y\) in the sense of 2-categories is a category in which an object is a triple \((x,y,\gamma)\), where \(\varphi(x)\xrightarrow\gamma\psi(y)\) is an isomorphism, and a map \((x,y;\gamma)\longrightarrow(x',y';\gamma')\) consists of a pair \(x\xrightarrow\alpha x'\), \(y\xrightarrow\beta y'\) with a commutative diagram \[ \xymatrix{\varphi(x)\ar[r]^{\varphi(\alpha)}\ar[d]_\gamma&\varphi(x')\ar[d]^{\gamma'}\\ \psi(y)\ar[r]_{\psi(\beta)}&\psi(y').} \]

One can rephrase the notions of (semi)homogeneous cofibered groupoid or smooth functor in terms of fibre-product of categories (in the sense of 2-category). Now let \(P:A\longrightarrow C_\Lambda\) be a cofibered category, and consider an object \(R_1\times_RR_2\) in \(C_\Lambda\). If we choose a direct image functor \((\pi_i)_*:A(R_1\times_RR_2)\longrightarrow A(R_i)\), where \(\pi_i:R_1\times_RR_2\longrightarrow R_i\) is the projection for \(i=1,2\), we obtain a functor \[A(R_1\times_RR_2)\longrightarrow A(R_1)\mathop{\times}\limits_{A(R)}^2A(R_2).\] It is clear from the definition of cofibered category that this functor, up to canonical natural equivalence, does not depend on the choice of direct image functors. It is now clear from the definition that a cofibered groupoid \(A\) over \(C_\Lambda\) is homogeneous over \(C_\Lambda\) if and only if \[A(R_1\times_RR_2)\longrightarrow A(R_1)\mathop{\times}\limits_{A(R)}^2A(R_2)\] is an equivalence of categories whenever \(R_1\longrightarrow R\) is surjective in \(C_\Lambda\), and \(A\) is semi-homogeneous if and only if \[[A(R_1\times_RR_2)]\longrightarrow[A(R_1)\mathop{\times}\limits_{A(R)}^2A(R_2)]\] is surjective whenever \(R_1\longrightarrow R\) is surjective in \(C_\Lambda\), and is bijective whenever \(R=k\) and \(R_1=k[\varepsilon]\). Now let \(\varphi:A\longrightarrow B\) be a functor of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). For each map \(R'\longrightarrow R\) in \(C_\Lambda\), \(\varphi\) induces a functor \[A(R')\longrightarrow A(R)\mathop{\times}\limits_{B(R)}^2B(R')\] uniquely determined up to a (canonical) natural equivalence. It is again trivial to see that \(\varphi\) is smooth if and only if the functor \[A(R')\longrightarrow A(R)\mathop{\times}\limits_{B(R)}^2B(R')\] is surjective on isomorphism classes of objects, whenever \(R'\longrightarrow R\) is surjective.

Remark 2.6(c). If \(A\) is a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), the functor \[A(k[V\times W])\longrightarrow A(k[V])\times A(k[W])\] is an equivalence of categories for any finite-dimensional vector spaces \(V,W\) over \(k\) (We recall our convention \(A(k)=\{e\}\)). In particular, we have \[\operatorname{Aut}_{A(k[V\times W])}(1_{V\times W})\simeq \operatorname{Aut}_{A(k[V])}(1_V)\times\operatorname{Aut}_{A(k[W])}(1_W),\] where \(1_V\) is the direct image of \(e\) under the unique map \(k\longrightarrow k[V]\) in \(C_\Lambda\). It follows that if \(A\) is homogeneous over \(C_\Lambda\), \(\operatorname{Aut}(1_\varepsilon)\) as well as \([A(k[\varepsilon])]\) are canonically provided with vector space structures over \(k\).

Remark 2.6(d). For any object \(R\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\), the cofibered (discrete) groupoid \(\underline h_R\) over \(C_\Lambda\) is homogeneous.

If \(A\) is semi-homogeneous, then so is \([A]\). However, the homogeneity of \(A\) does not imply in general the homogeneity of \([A]\). Indeed we have

Proposition 2.7.

Let \(A\) be a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\). The following statements are equivalent.

  1. (i) \([A]\) is homogeneous i.e. \[[A(R'\times_RS)]\longrightarrow[A(R')]\mathop{\times}_{[A(R)]}[A(S)]\] is bijective for every small extension \(R'\longrightarrow R\).
  2. (ii) For every small prolongation \(a'\longrightarrow a\) in \(A\), the map \[\operatorname{Aut}_{A(R')}(a')\longrightarrow\operatorname{Aut}_{A(R)}(a)\] is surjective, where \(R'=P(a')\), \(R=P(a)\).

Proof. (i) \(\Rightarrow\) (ii) Let \(R'\xrightarrow{f}R\) be a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\) and let \(a'\longrightarrow a\) be a \(f\)-map in \(A\). Given \(\tau\in\operatorname{Aut}_{A(R)}(a)\), we consider \(a'\times_a a'\) and \(a'\times_a a'_\tau\), where \(a'_\tau\longrightarrow a\) denotes the composite map \(a'\longrightarrow a\xrightarrow{\tau}a\). Since \[[A(R'\times_RR')]\longrightarrow[A(R')]\mathop{\times}_{[A(R)]}[A(R')]]\] is bijective, we conclude the existence of an isomorphism \(a'\times_a a'\xrightarrow{u}a'\times_a a'_\tau\), and \(u\) induces, via the projection on the second factor, an isomorphism \(a'\xrightarrow{\tau'}a'\) which is a lifting of \(\tau\).

(ii) \(\Rightarrow\) (i) Let \(a_i''\;(i=1,2)\) be objects in \(A(R'\times_RS)\) which have the same image under \([A(R'\times_RS)]\longrightarrow[A(R')]\times_{[A(R)]}[A(S)]\). We then obtain a diagram of solid arrows \[ \xymatrix{&a_2''\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&&R'\times_RS\ar[dl]\ar[dr]\\ a_2'\ar@{<-->}[r]&a_1'&b_2&R'&S\\ &a_1''\ar[ul]\ar[ur]&&&R\ar[ul]\ar[ur]} \] where \(\bar\tau:a_1\longrightarrow a_2\) is the induced isomorphism from \(\tau\) so that the square box on the lower right corner is commutative. If the condition (ii) is verified, one can always replace the given isomorphism \(a_1'\simeq a_2'\) so that the square box on the left corner is also commutative. Since \(a_i''=a_i'\times_{a_i}b_i\) for \(i=1,2\) by 2.6(a), we obtain the isomorphism \(a_1''\simeq a_2''\). This proves that \[[A(R'\times_RS)]\longrightarrow[A(R')]\mathop{\times}_{[A(R)]}[A(S)]\] is injective, and hence is bijective since \(A\) is homogeneous by hypothesis.

Now let \(A\) be a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), and let \(R'\xrightarrow{f}R\) be a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\). We have a canonical map

\[\widetilde f:R'\mathop{\times}_R R'\longrightarrow k[\operatorname{Ker}f] \quad\hbox{given by}\quad (r'_1,r'_2)\longmapsto r'_1(0)+(r'_1-r'_2),\]

and \(1\times\widetilde f:R'\mathop{\times}_R R'\longrightarrow R'\times_k k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) is an isomorphism. Given any two \(f\)-maps \(a_i\to a\;(i=1,2)\) in \(A\), \(a_1\times_a a_2\) is an object over \(R'\times_RR'\) and in turn we may consider \[\tau(a_1,a_2)=(\widetilde f)_*(a_1\times_a a_2)\] which is an object in \(A(k[\operatorname{Ker}f])\). Since \(1\times\widetilde f:R'\times_RR'\longrightarrow R'\times_k k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) is an isomorphism, we have the isomorphism \[a_1\times_a a_2\simeq a_1\times\tau(a_1,a_2)\] compatible with the projections on the first factor. The statement 1.5(2) takes a more definite form for a homogeneous groupoid.

Lemma 2.8.

Let \(A\) be a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), and \(a_i\xrightarrow{\alpha_i}a\;(i=1,2)\) \(f\)-maps in \(A\). Then

  1. There exists \(\rho:a_1\to a_2\) in \(A\) with \(\alpha_2\cdot\rho=\alpha_1\) if and only if there exists an arrow \(a_1\to\tau(a_1,a_2)\) in \(A\).
  2. There exists \(\rho:a_1\to a_2\) with \(\alpha_2\cdot\rho=\alpha_1\) such that \(P(\rho)=\operatorname{id}_{R'}\) if and only if \(\tau(a_1,a_2)=0\) in \([A(k[\operatorname{Ker}f])]\).

Proof. We prove both at the same time. There exists \(\rho:a_1\to a_2\) in \(A\) with \(\alpha_2\cdot\rho=\alpha_1\) (such that \(P(\rho)=\operatorname{id}_{R'}\)) \(\Leftrightarrow\) there exists \(h:a_1\to a_1\times_a a_2\) in \(A\) with \(\operatorname{pr}_1\cdot h=\operatorname{id}_{a_1}\) (such that \(P(h)=\) diagonal map) \(\Leftrightarrow\) there exists \(g:a_1\to a_1\times\tau(a_1,a_2)\) in \(A\) with \(\operatorname{pr}_1\cdot g=\operatorname{id}_{a_1}\) (such that \(P(g):R'\to R'\times_k k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) is given by \(r'\mapsto(r',r'(0))\)) \(\Leftrightarrow\) there exists an arrow \(\gamma:a_1\to\tau(a_1,a_2)\) in \(A\) (such that \(P(\gamma):R'\to k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) is given by \(r'\mapsto r'(0)\)). However, there exists an arrow \(\gamma:a_1\to\tau(a_1,a_2)\) in \(A\) such that \(P(\gamma):R'\to k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) is factored through \(R'\to k\to k[\operatorname{Ker}f]\) if and only if \(\tau(a_1,a_2)=0\).

Proposition 2.9.

(a) Let \(A\xrightarrow{\varphi}B\xrightarrow{\psi}C\) be functors of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). Assume that

  1. (i) \(B\) is semi-homogeneous and \(C\) is homogeneous;
  2. (ii) \(\psi(k[\epsilon]):[B(k[\epsilon])]\to[C(k[\epsilon])]\) is injective.

Then the composite functor \(\psi\circ\varphi\) is smooth if and only if \(\varphi\) and \(\psi\) are both smooth.

(b) Let \(\varphi:A\to B\) be a smooth functor of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). If \(A\) is semi-homogeneous and \(B\) is homogeneous, then \(\widehat\varphi:\widehat A\to\widehat B\) is smooth (i.e., for any surjective map \(b'\xrightarrow{\beta}\varphi(a)\) in \(\widehat B\), there exists \(a'\xrightarrow{\alpha}a\) in \(\widehat A\) and a \(\widehat C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism \(\rho:\varphi(a')\xrightarrow{\sim}b'\) such that \(\beta\cdot\rho=\varphi(\alpha)\)).

Proof. (a) Assume that the composite functor \(\psi\circ\varphi\) is smooth. If \(\varphi\) is smooth, then so is \(\psi\) by 2.3(b), and therefore it suffices to show that \(\varphi\) is smooth. Let a small prolongation \(b'\xrightarrow{\beta}\varphi(a)\) in \(B\) be given. Since the composite functor \(\psi\circ\varphi\) is smooth, there exists an arrow \(a'\xrightarrow{\alpha}a\) in \(A\) and a \(C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism \(\rho:\psi(b')\longrightarrow\psi\varphi(a')\) with a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{\psi(b')\ar[r]^\rho\ar[dr]_{\psi(\beta)}&\psi\varphi(a')\ar[d]^{\psi\varphi(\alpha)}\\ &\psi\varphi(a).}\] Since \(B\) is semi-homogeneous, we can choose a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&b''\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&&&R'\times_RR'\ar[dl]\ar[dr]\\ b'\ar[dr]_\beta&&\varphi(a')\ar[dl]&\text{above}&R'\ar[dr]&&R'\ar[dl]\\ &\varphi(a)&&&&R} \] where \(R=P(a)\), \(R'=P(b')=P(a')\). Since \(C\) is homogeneous, \[\psi(b'')=\psi(b')\mathop{\times}_{\psi\varphi(a)}\psi\varphi(a')\quad\text{by 2.6(a)},\] and it follows from 2.8(2) that \[0=\tau(\psi\varphi(a'),\psi(b'))=(\widetilde f)_*\psi(b''),\] and hence \((\widetilde f)_*(b'')=0\) by the hypothesis (ii). It follows from 1.5(2) that there exists a map \(\gamma:b'\to\varphi(a')\) such that \(\beta=\varphi(\alpha)\cdot\gamma\).

(b) Let a surjective map \(\beta:b'\to\varphi(a)\) in \(\widehat B\) be given. This means that we are given a sequence of commutative diagrams

\[\xymatrix{\cdots\ar[r]&b'_3\ar[r]^{\beta'_2}\ar[d]^{\beta_3}&b'_2\ar[r]^{\beta'_1}\ar[d]^{\beta_2}&b'_1\ar[d]^{\beta_1}\\ \cdots\ar[r]&\varphi(a_3)\ar[r]^{\varphi(\alpha_2)}&\varphi(a_2)\ar[r]^{\varphi(\alpha_1)}&\varphi(a_1)}\] in \(B\), where \(\alpha_\nu,\beta_\nu,\beta'_\nu\) are surjective for all \(\nu\), such that \(\beta=\varprojlim_\nu\beta_\nu\). To show that \(\widehat\varphi\) is smooth, it suffices to prove that a commutative diagram below of solid arrows in which \(\alpha_n,\beta_n,\beta_{n+1},\beta'_n\) are surjective can be completed into a commutative diagram including dotted arrows.

\[\xymatrix{\varphi(a'_{n+1})\ar@{-->}[r]^{\varphi( )}\ar@{-->}[dr]\ar@{-->}[ddr]_{\varphi( )}& \varphi(a'_n)\ar[dr]\ar[ddr]^{\varphi(\alpha'_n)}\\ &b'_{n+1}\ar[r]^{\beta'_n}\ar[d]^{\beta_{n+1}}&b'_n\ar[d]^{\beta_n}\\ &\varphi(a_{n+1})\ar[r]_{\varphi(\alpha_n)}&\varphi(a_n)}\] Now \(A\) is, by hypotheses, semi-homogeneous and \(\alpha_n:a'_{n+1}\to a_n\) is surjective, and therefore there exists a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&a'\ar[dl]_\gamma\ar[dr]^{\gamma'}&&&R_{n+1}\times_{R_n}R'_n\ar[dl]\ar[dr]\\ a_{n+1}\ar[dr]_{\alpha_n}&&a'_n\ar[dl]^{\alpha'_n}&\text{in }A\text{ over}&R_{n+1}\ar[dr]&&R'_n\ar[dl]\\ &a_n&&&&R_n} \] in \(C_\Lambda\). The hypothesis that \(B\) is homogeneous entails that \[\xymatrix{&\varphi(a')\ar[dl]_{\varphi(\gamma)}\ar[dr]^{\varphi(\gamma')}&\\ \varphi(a_{n+1})\ar[dr]_{\varphi(\alpha_n)}&&\varphi(a'_n)\ar[dl]^{\varphi(\alpha'_n)}\\ &\varphi(a_n)&} \] is a diagram of a fibre-product in \(B\), and therefore we obtain a unique map \(\delta:b'_{n+1}\to\varphi(a')\) in \(B\) with an appropriate commutativity. Since \(\beta_{n+1},\beta'_n\) are both surjective, so is \(\delta\) and therefore, since \(\varphi\) is smooth, there exists \(a'_{n+1}\xrightarrow{\alpha'}a'\) in \(A\) and a \(C_\Lambda\)-isomorphism \(\varphi(a'_{n+1})\xrightarrow{\rho}b'_{n+1}\) such that \(\delta\cdot\rho=\varphi(\alpha')\). We then obtain the commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{\varphi(a'_{n+1})\ar[r]^{\varphi(\gamma'\cdot\alpha')}\ar[dr]^\rho \ar[ddr]_{\varphi(\gamma\cdot\alpha')}&\varphi(a'_n)\ar[dr]\ar[ddr]^{\varphi(\alpha'_n)}\\ &b'_{n+1}\ar[r]&b'_n\ar[d]\\ &\varphi(a_{n+1})\ar[r]&\varphi(a_n).}\]

Corollary 2.10.

(a) Let \(\varphi:A\to B\) be a smooth functor of cofibered groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). Let \(\xi\) be a versal formal object of \(A\), and \(\eta\) a minimally-versal formal object of \(B\). If \(B\) is homogeneous, then for any map \(\beta:\eta\to\varphi(\xi)\) in \(\widehat B\), \(P(\xi)\) is a formal power-series ring over \(P(\eta)\) through \[P(\beta):P(\eta)\to P(\varphi(\xi))=P(\xi).\]

(b) Let \(A\) be a homogeneous cofibered groupoid over \(C_\Lambda\), and let \(\xi,\eta\) be versal formal objects of \(A\). If \(\eta\) is minimal, then for any map \(\alpha:\eta\to\xi\) in \(\widehat A\), \(P(\xi)\) is a formal power-series ring over \(P(\eta)\) through \(P(\alpha):P(\eta)\to P(\xi)\).

Proof. (a) A map \(\beta:\eta\to\varphi(\xi)\) induces a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{h_{P(\xi)}\ar[r]^{\widetilde\beta}\ar[d]^{\widetilde\xi}&h_{P(\eta)}\ar[d]^{\widetilde\eta}\\ A\ar[r]_\varphi&B.}\] Since \(\widetilde\xi,\varphi\) are smooth, \(\widetilde\eta\widetilde\beta= \varphi\widetilde\xi\) is smooth. On the other hand, \(\widetilde\eta(k[\epsilon]):h_{P(\eta)}(k[\epsilon])\to[B(k[\epsilon])]\) is an isomorphism and therefore \(\widetilde\beta\) is smooth by 2.9(a), which means that \(P(\xi)\) is a formal power-series ring over \(P(\eta)\).

(b) follows from (a) by setting \(A=B\).

Finally we consider the question of “representability” of a cofibered groupoid. Let \(C\) be a fixed category. Each object \(R\) in \(C\) defines a functor \(h_R:C\to(\mathrm{Ens})\) given by \(h_R(S)=\operatorname{Hom}_C(R,S)\), and in turn we obtain a functor \(h:C^o\to\operatorname{Hom}(C,(\mathrm{Ens}))\) which is fully-faithful. A “cocategory in \(C\)” is a diagram \[h_{R_1}\mathop{\times}_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1}\xrightarrow{c}h_{R_1} \mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow{s_1}\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow[s_2]{}}}h_{R_0}, \qquad s_i\cdot\epsilon=\text{identity for }i=1,2\] (where \(h_{R_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1}=(h_{R_1})_{s_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}(h_{R_1})_{s_2}\)) such that

  1. (i) \(c\) is associative and is compatible with \(s_i\) for \(i=1,2\);
  2. (ii) the two composites \[h_{R_1}\xrightarrow{\sim}h_{R_0}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1} \xrightarrow{\epsilon\times1}h_{R_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1}\xrightarrow c h_{R_1},\] \[h_{R_1}\xrightarrow{\sim}h_{R_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_0} \xrightarrow{1\times\epsilon}h_{R_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1}\xrightarrow c h_{R_1}\] are both identities.

If \((R_0,R_1,s_1,s_2,\epsilon,c)\) is a cocategory in \(C\), then for an object \(S\) in \(C\), we can define a category \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)\) by setting \[\operatorname{ob}h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)=\operatorname{Hom}_C(R_0,S),\qquad \operatorname{Fl}h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)=\operatorname{Hom}_C(R_1,S)\] in which the composition rule of arrows is given by \(c:h_{R_1}\times_{h_{R_0}}h_{R_1}\to h_{R_1}\). Then \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}:C\to(\text{categories})\) is a functor and in turn defines a cofibered category \(\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) over \(C\). For instance, any object \(R\) in \(C\) defines a discrete cocategory \(h_R=h_{(R,R,s_1,s_2,\epsilon,c)}\) by setting \(s_1,s_2,\epsilon,c\) to be all equal to the identity map. A homomorphism \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\to(R'_0,R'_1,\ldots)\) of cocategories in \(C\) is a pair of maps \(h_{R_0}\to h_{R'_0}\), \(h_{R_1}\to h_{R'_1}\), in \(C\) such that the induced map \[h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)\to h_{(R'_0,R'_1,\ldots)}(S)\] is a functor for every object \(S\) in \(C\). For instance, each cocategory \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) is provided with a homomorphism \(\epsilon:h_{R_0}\to h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) of cocategories. A cogroupoid in \(C\) is by definition a cocategory \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(C\) such that \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)\) is a groupoid for every object \(S\) in \(C\). In other words, a cogroupoid in \(C\) is a cocategory \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(C\) such that \(\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is a cofibered category in groupoids over \(C\).

In practice, a cocategory or cogroupoid occurs in a following fashion: Let \(P:F\to C\) be a cofibered category, and let \(\xi\) be a fixed object in \(F\). Set \(R_0=P(\xi)\) and let \((R_0,C)\) be the category of arrows in \(C\) with the source \(R_0\). Let \(P:F'\to(R_0,C)\) be the pull-back of \(F\) under the canonical functor \((R_0,C)\to C\), and choose a cocartesian section \(\xi^\#\) of \(F'\) over \((R_0,C)\) such that \(\xi^\#(\operatorname{id}_{R_0})=\xi\). (This simply means that we choose, for each arrow \(R_0\xrightarrow f S\) in \(C\), a cocartesian \(f\)-map \(\xi\to\xi^\#(f)\).) Define the functor \[\operatorname{Hom}_{\xi^\#}:(R_0\amalg R_0,C)\to(\mathrm{Ens})\] given by \[X=(f_1,f_2)\longmapsto\operatorname{Hom}_{F(S)}(\xi^\#(f_1),\xi^\#(f_2))\] where \(S\) = the target of \(f_1\) (= the target of \(f_2\)). We note that this functor does not depend on the choice of \(\xi^\#\) up to (canonical) natural equivalence. Henceforth, by abuse of notation, we shall simply write \(\xi\) for \(\xi^\#\). If this functor is representable by an object \(X_0=(R_0\mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow{s_1}\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow[s_2]{}}}R_1)\) in \((R_0\amalg R_0,C)\), i.e. if there exists \(R_0\)-map \(\varphi:\xi(s_1)\to\xi(s_2)\) in \(F\) such that the induced map \[\widetilde\varphi:\operatorname{Hom}_{(R_0\amalg R_0,C)}(X_0,X)\to\operatorname{Hom}_\xi(X)\] is bijective for all \(X\) in \((R_0\amalg R_0,C)\), then the object \(X_0\) defines a cocategory \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(C\) in a natural way. Indeed, a triple of arrows \[R_0\mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow u\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow v\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow w{}}}S\] in \(C\) defines a composition \[\operatorname{Hom}_{F(S)}(\xi(v),\xi(w))\times\operatorname{Hom}_{F(S)}(\xi(u),\xi(v))\to\operatorname{Hom}_{F(S)}(\xi(u),\xi(w))\] and in turn yields the composition rule \[h_{R_1}(S)\mathop{\times}_{h_{R_0}(S)}h_{R_1}(S)\to h_{R_1}(S)\] depending functorially on \(S\). Consequently the functor \(\operatorname{Hom}_\xi\) defines the cocategory \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(C\) and in turn defines a cofibered category \(\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) in \(C\). By definition, \[\operatorname{ob}\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}=\coprod_{S\in\operatorname{ob}(C)}\operatorname{Hom}_C(R_0,S)\] (disjoint union for \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C)\)), and an arrow \((R_0\xrightarrow f S)\to(R_0\xrightarrow{f'}S')\) is a pair \(S\xrightarrow h S'\) and \(R_1\xrightarrow u S'\) in \(C\) such that \(u\cdot s_1=h\cdot f\) and \(u\cdot s_2=f'\), and the structural functor \(P:\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\to C\) is given by \((R_0\xrightarrow f S)\mapsto S\). Now the pair \((\xi,\varphi)\) defines the functor \[(\xi,\varphi):\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\to F\quad\text{over }C\] by \((R_0\xrightarrow f S)\mapsto\xi(f)\) and \[[(R_0\xrightarrow f S)\xrightarrow{(h,u)}(R_0\xrightarrow{f'}S')]\longmapsto\text{ the composite map }\xi(f)\xrightarrow{h_\#}\xi(hf)\xrightarrow{u(\varphi)}\xi(f').\]

We note that, for each object \(S\) in \(C\), the functor \[(\xi,\varphi)(S):h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(S)\to F(S)\] is fully-faithful by virtue of the definition of \((R_0\mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow{s_1}\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow[s_2]{}}}R_1)\) coming from the representability of the functor \(\operatorname{Hom}_\xi\). Consequently the functor \((\xi,\varphi):\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\to F\) is an \(C\)-equivalence if and only if \(\xi\) is a quasi-initial object of \(F\) over \(C\) i.e., for every object \(a\) in \(F\) there exists a cocartesian arrow \(\xi\to a\). Instead of this functor \(\operatorname{Hom}_\xi\) one may also consider the functor \[\operatorname{Isom}_\xi:(R_0\amalg R_0,C)\to(\mathrm{Ens})\] given by \(X=(f_1,f_2)\mapsto\operatorname{Isom}_{F(S)}(\xi(f_1),\xi(f_2))\) where \(S\) is the target of \(f_1\) (= the target of \(f_2\)). If this functor is representable by an object \((R_0\mathrel{\substack{\xrightarrow{s_1}\\[-.6ex]\xrightarrow[s_2]{}}}R_1)\) in \((R_0\amalg R_0,C)\), then it defines a cogroupoid \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(C\) together with the functor \((\xi,\varphi):\underline h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\to F\), which, when \(F\) is a cofibered groupoid, a \(C\)-equivalence if and only if \(\xi\) is a quasi-initial object of \(F\) over \(C\).

Definition and proposition 2.11.

A cogroupoid \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) is called smooth if any one of the following equivalent conditions holds:

  1. (i) \(R_1\) is a formal power-series ring over \(R_0\) via \(s_1\) (and \(s_2\)).
  2. (ii) \(\epsilon:h_{R_0}\to h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is smooth over \(C_\Lambda\).

A minimally smooth cogroupoid or a normalized smooth cogroupoid in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) is a smooth cogroupoid \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\) such that \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(k[\epsilon])\) is totally disconnected i.e., \(s_1(k[\epsilon])=s_2(k[\epsilon])\).

Proof. \(\epsilon:h_{R_0}\to h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is smooth over \(C_\Lambda\) \(\Leftrightarrow\) given any surjective map \((S',f')\xrightarrow{(1,u)}(S,f)\) in \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\), there exists a lifting \(f''\) of \(f\) to \(S'\) together with an isomorphism \((S',f'')\xrightarrow{(1,u')}(S',f')\) given \(u\in h_{R_1}(S)\) and a lifting \(f'\) of \(s_1(u)\) to \(S'\), there exists a lifting \(u'\) of \(u\) to \(S'\) such that \(s_1(u')=f'\), where \(S'\to S\) is an arbitrary surjective map in \(C_\Lambda\) \(\Leftrightarrow s_1:h_{R_1}\to h_{R_0}\) is smooth over \(C_\Lambda\).

Proposition 2.12.

Let \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) be a cogroupoid in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\).

(a) If \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) is smooth, then \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is homogeneous.

(b) If \(s_1(k[\epsilon])=s_2(k[\epsilon])\), then the following statements are equivalent:

  1. (i) \((R_0,R_1,\ldots)\) is smooth
  2. (ii) \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is homogeneous
  3. (iii) \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is semi-homogeneous.

(c) If \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is semi-homogeneous, then it is equivalent to \(h_{(S_0,S_1,\ldots)}\) for some minimally smooth cogroupoid \(S_*\).

Proof. (a) Consider a diagram \[\xymatrix{(S_1,f_1)\ar[dr]^{(h_1,u_1)}&&(S_2,f_2)\ar[dl]_{(h_2,u_2)}\\&(S,f)&}\] in \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\), where \(h_2:S_2\to S\) is surjective in \(C_\Lambda\). Since \(s_1:h_{R_1}\to h_{R_0}\) is smooth by hypothesis, \(u_1^{-1}u_2\) can be lifted to some \(v\in h_{R_1}(S_2)\) such that \(s_1(v)=f_2\). Since \(h_2\cdot s_2(v)=h_1\cdot f_1\), \(f_1\times s_2(v)\in h_{R_0}(S_1\times_S S_2)\) is defined and we obtain the commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{&(S_1\times_S S_2,f_1\times s_2(v))\ar[dl]_{\pi_1}\ar[dr]^{(\pi_2,v^{-1})}&\(S_1,f_1)\ar[dr]_{(h_1,u_1)}&&(S_2,f_2)\ar[dl]^{(h_2,u_2)}\\&(S,f)&}\] We claim that the above diagram defines a fibre-product \((S_1,f_1)\times_{(S,f)}(S_2,f_2)\) in \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\). Indeed, for any object \((T,g)\) in \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\), we have \[\begin{aligned}\operatorname{Hom}((T,g),(S_1\times_S S_2,f_1\times s_2(v))) &=\{(h'_1\times h'_2,w_1,w_2)\mid s_1(w_1)=s_1(w_2)=g,\\&\quad s_2(w_1)=f_1,\ s_2(w_2)=s_2(v)\}\\ &=\{(h'_1\times h'_2,w_1,w_2)\mid h'_1w_1=h'_2w_2,\\&\quad s_1(w_1)=h'_1g,\ s_1(w_2)=h'_2g,\ s_2(w_1)=f_1,\ s_2(w_2)=s_2(v)\}\\ &=\{(h'_1\times h'_2,v,w_1,w_2)\mid u_1^{-1}u_2h_2(v^{-1}w_2)=h_1w_1,\\&\quad s_1(w_1)=h'_1g,\ s_1(w_2)=h'_2g,\ s_2(w_1)=f_1,\ s_2(w_2)=s_2(v)\}. \end{aligned}\]

(b) It suffices to show (iii) \(\Rightarrow\) (i). Since \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is semi-homogeneous and \(\dim[h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(k[\epsilon])]=\dim h_{R_0}(k[\epsilon])<\infty\), \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) admits a minimally versal formal object. On the other hand, \((R_0,1_{R_0})\) is a quasi-initial minimal object of \(\widehat h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) since \(h_{R_0}(k[\epsilon])\to[h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(k[\epsilon])]\) is bijective, and therefore \((R_0,1_{R_0})\) is a minimally versal formal object of \(\widehat h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) by 1.10(11), which means that \(\epsilon:h_{R_0}\to\widehat h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) is minimally smooth.

(c) Let \((S_0,\xi_0)\) be a minimally versal formal object for \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}\) (1.11). For any object \(\xi:R_0\to T\) in \(h_{(R_0,R_1,\ldots)}(T)\), the functor \(\operatorname{Isom}(\xi,\xi)\) from \(C_\Lambda/T\amalg_\Lambda T\) to (sets) which to any \(R\) associates the sets of isomorphisms between the two images of \(\xi\) on \(R:R_0\xrightarrow\xi T\to T\amalg_\Lambda T\to R\), is represented by \(R_1\amalg_{R_0\amalg_\Lambda R_0}(T\amalg_\Lambda T)\). Proof. Lemma 2.15.

Let \(A,B\) be discrete groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\) and \(\varphi:A\to B\) a minimally smooth functor. If \(A,B\) are homogeneous, then \(\varphi\) is a \(C_\Lambda\)-equivalence.

Proof. We must show that \(\varphi(S):A(S)\to B(S)\) is bijective for all \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)\). We note that \(\varphi(S)\) is surjective for all \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)\) and bijective for \(S=k[\epsilon]\). We argue by induction on the length of \(S\). Let \(S'\xrightarrow f S\) be a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\). 3. The coherent sheaves \(D_X^i\)

One of the nice functors which is usually not pro-representable but nevertheless admits a formal versal object is the deformation functor. To this end we recall a few basic facts on commutative ring extensions and its obstruction theory. This section contains nothing new (possibly except in its systematic approach via Grothendieck cohomology on site). A purpose of this section is to make this expose as much self-contained. (Indeed we prove here more than we need later). The basic facts are stated in theorem 3.12. Throughout rings are meant to be commutative rings with unit.

Given a ring \(R\) we denote by \(C_R\) the category of \(R\)-algebras. For each object \(A\) in \(C_R\) we set \[\operatorname{Cov}\(A\)=\text{ the set of all surjective arrows }\varphi:A'\to A \text{ in }C_R\text{ with target }A.\] It clearly defines a pretopology and in turn we obtain a site \(C_R\). For each object \(A\) in \(C_R\), we shall denote by \(C_{A|R}\) the category of arrows in \(C_R\) with target \(A\). We may and shall provide the category \(C_{A|R}\) with the topology induced from the forgetful functor \(j_A:C_{A|R}\to C_R\). We note that the category \(C_{A|R}\) has a final object (\(1_A:A\to A\)) and an initial object (\(i_A:R\to A\)), and admits a fibre-product as well as coproduct. Henceforth we shall denote the object (\(B\to A\)) in \(C_{A|R}\), by abuse of notation, simply by \(B\) in case when there is no possible confusion. Given an abelian category \(\mathcal C\), a \(\mathcal C\)-valued sheaf on \(C_{A|R}\) is, by definition, a functor \(F:C_{A|R}^o\to\mathcal C\) such that, for any surjective arrow \(B'\to B\) in \(C_{A|R}\), \[0\to F(B)\to F(B')\rightrightarrows F(B'\mathbin{\mathop{\times}\limits_B}B')\] is exact. The additive presheaves are those which preserve coproduct i.e., (F\(B_1\otimes_R B_2\)=F(B_1)\oplus F\(B_2\)). For example, an \(A\)-module \(M\) defines the functor \[\operatorname{Der}_R\(-,M\):C_{A|R}^o\to(A\text{-mod})\] given by (B\mapsto\operatorname{Der}_R\(B,M\)) = \(R\)-linear derivations of \(B\) with values in \(M\) regarded as \(B\)-module via the structural map \(B\to A\), and it is clearly an additive sheaf on \(C_{A|R}^o\), where (\(A\text{-mod}\)) denotes the category of \(A\)-modules. A presheaf \(F\) on \(C_{A|R}\) with values in (\(A\text{-mod}\)) is called “finite type” if (F\(B\)) is an \(A\)-module of finite type whenever \(B\) is an \(R\)-algebra of finite type. For example, if \(A\) is noetherian and \(M\) is an \(A\)-module of finite type, then (\operatorname{Der}_R\(-,M\)) is an additive sheaf of finite type on the site \(C_{A|R}\).

A few more words on notations and basic facts in homological algebra:

3.1\(a\) Throughout the rest of this section, we shall denote by \(\widetilde C_{A|R}\) (or \(C_{A|R}^{\vee}\)) the category of sheaves (or presheaves) with values in (\(A\text{-mod}\)). We have the (left exact) injection functor \(i_{A|R}:\widetilde C_{A|R}\to C_{A|R}^{\vee}\), and the associated-sheaf \(exact\) functor \(a_{A|R}:C_{A|R}^{\vee}\to\widetilde C_{A|R}\) which is adjoint to the functor \(i_{A|R}\). An object (B\in\operatorname{ob}\(C_{A|R}\)) induces a continuous functor \(j:C_{B|R}\to C_{A|R}\) and in turn a commutative diagram

\[\begin{CD} \widetilde C_{A|R} @>{\widetilde j}>> \widetilde C_{B|R}\\ @V{i_{A|R}}VV @VVV\\ C_{A|R}^{\vee} @>{j^{\vee}}>> C_{B|R}^{\vee}. \end{CD}\tag{*}\]

where the restriction functor \(\widetilde j\) (as well as \(j^{\vee}\)) is an exact functor, since the site \(C_{B|R}\) is nothing but the category of arrows in \(C_{A|R}\) having target \(B\), with the induced topology. A ring homomorphism \(S\to R\) induces a continuous functor \(\beta:C_{A|R}\to C_{A|S}\), and in turn a commutative diagram

\[\begin{CD} \widetilde C_{A|S} @>{\widetilde\beta}>> \widetilde C_{A|R}\\ @V{i_{A|S}}VV @VVV\\ C_{A|S}^{\vee} @>{\beta^{\vee}}>> C_{A|R}^{\vee}. \end{CD}\tag{**}\]

Note that \(\widetilde\beta\) is not necessarily an exact functor.

3.1\(b\) The right derived functor of the injection functor \(i_{A|R}:\widetilde C_{A|R}\to C_{A|R}^{\vee}\) will be denoted by \(\underline H_{A|R}^{\bullet}\). For each sheaf \(F\) on \(C_{A|R}\), we set \[H^o\(A|R,F\)=[\underline H_{A|R}^o\(F\)]\(A\).\] For any \(B'\to B\) in (\operatorname{Cov}\(B\)), let us define the functor \[H^o(\(B'\to B\),-):C_{A|R}^{\vee}\to(A\text{-mod})\] by \[H^o(\(B'\to B\),F)=\operatorname{Ker}\bigl(F(B')\rightrightarrows F(B'\mathbin{\mathop{\times}\limits_B}B')\bigr).\] The right derived functors of (H^o(\(B'\to B\),-)) will be denoted by (H^{\bullet}(\(B'\to B\),-)). One knows that the (H^{\bullet}(\(B'\to B\),F)) are the cohomology groups of the \(Čech\) semi-simplicial module \[F(B')\rightrightarrows F\(B'\mathbin{\mathop{\times}\limits_B}B'\) \triplerightarrow F(B'\mathbin{\mathop{\times}\limits_B}B' \mathbin{\mathop{\times}\limits_B}B')\cdots.\] The right derived functor of \(\underline H_{A|R}^{\vee o}:C_{A|R}^{\vee}\to C_{A|R}\) will be denoted by \(\underline H_{A|R}^{\vee\bullet}\), where \[[\underline H_{A|R}^{\vee o}\(F\)]\(B\)= \varinjlim_{\{B'\to B\}\in\operatorname{Cov}\(B\)}H^o(\(B'\to B\),F).\] For each presheaf \(F\) on \(C_{A|R}\), we set \[\check H^{\bullet}\(A|R,F\)=[\underline H_{A|R}^{\vee\bullet}\(F\)]\(A\) =\varinjlim_{\{A'\to A\}\in\operatorname{Cov}\(A\)}H^{\bullet}(\(A'\to A\),F).\] For any sheaf \(F\) on \(C_{A|R}\), we have the usual spectral sequence \[\check H^p(A|R,\underline H_{A|R}^q\(F\))\Rightarrow H^{p+q}(A|R,F)\] where we note that (\check H^o(A|R,\underline H^q\(F\))=0) for all \(q>0\). In particular \[\begin{cases} \check H^1\(A|R,F\)=H^1\(A|R,F\),\\ 0\to\check H^2(A|R,F)\to H^2(A|R,F)\to \check H^1(A|R,\underline H_{A|R}^1\(F\))\to\check H^3\(A|R,F\) \quad\text{is exact.} \end{cases}\]

3.1\(c\) If \(B\) is an object in \(C_{A|R}\), the commutative diagram \(*\) in 2.1\(a\) together with the fact that \(\widetilde j\) as well as \(j^{\vee}\) are exact functors yields the canonical isomorphism \[H^{\bullet}\(B|R,\widetilde jF\)=[\underline H_{A|R}^{\bullet}\(F\)]\(B\).\] On the other hand, a ring homomorphism \(S\to R\) induces the commutative diagram \(**\) in 2.1\(a\). Now \(\widetilde\beta\) is not necessarily an exact functor. However, \(\beta\) preserves a fibre-product, and thus we obtain a spectral sequence \[\underline H_{A|R}^p(R^q\widetilde\beta F)\Rightarrow \beta\underline H_{A|S}^{p+q}\(F\),\quad\text{i.e.,}\quad H^p(A|R,R^q\widetilde\beta F)\Rightarrow H^{p+q}\(A|S,F\).\] Since we have the canonical isomorphism \(\widetilde\beta=a_{A|R}\widetilde\beta^o i_{A|S}\) and \(\beta^{\#}=a_{A|R}\beta^{\vee}\) is an exact functor, we have (R^q\widetilde\beta=\beta^{\#}\underline H_{A|S}^q\(F\)). Thus the spectral sequence above can now be written as \[H^p(A|R,\beta^{\#}\underline H_{A|S}^q\(F\))\Rightarrow H^{p+q}\(A|S,F\).\]

Proof: (1) If (0\to M'\to M\to M''\to0) is an exact sequence of (A)-modules, then for any polynomial algebra (P) over (R), (0\to\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M')\to\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)\to\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M'')\to0) is exact and hence (0\to\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M')\to\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M)\to\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M'')\to0) is an exact sequence of sheaves by 3.3(c).

(2) (operatorname{Der}_S(-,M)) is an additive sheaf on (C_{A\mid S}), and \[\operatorname{Der}_R(B,M)=\operatorname{Ker}(\operatorname{Der}_S(B,M)\to\operatorname{Der}_S(R,M))\] for every (B\in\operatorname{ob}(C_{A\mid R})). Therefore our assertion follows from 3.7(c).

(3) Let (S:C_{A\mid R}\to C_{A\otimes_RS\mid S}) be the functor given by (X\mapsto X\otimes_RS). Then \[\widetilde S\operatorname{Der}_S(-,M)=\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M)\] and hence our assertion follows from 3.7(a).

(4) Follows from 3.9. Indeed, (F=\operatorname{Der}_R(-,N)) is an additive sheaf on (C_{S^{-1}A\mid R}), and (F_A\in\operatorname{ob}(C_{S^{-1}A\mid A})) is also additive since \[F_A(X)=\operatorname{Ker}(F(X)\to F(A))=\operatorname{Ker}(\operatorname{Der}_R(X,N)\to\operatorname{Der}_R(A,N))=\operatorname{Der}_A(X,N),\] i.e., (F_A=\operatorname{Der}_A(-,N)).

(5) The first assertion follows from 3.8(a). Now assume that \(\operatorname{Tor}_i^R(A,A)=0\) for all \(i>0\) and that \[\Delta:\operatorname{Spec}(A)\to\operatorname{Spec}(A)\mathbin{\mathop\times\limits_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}}\operatorname{Spec}(A)\] is an open immersion i.e., \((A\otimes_RA)_{\varphi^{-1}(\mathfrak m)}\to A_{\mathfrak m}\) is an isomorphism for all maximal ideals \(\mathfrak m\) of (A), where \(\varphi:A\otimes_RA\to A\) is the multiplication map. Then the first assertion combined with 3.12(4) entails that the diagonal map \[\Delta_{\mathfrak m}:D^i(A\mid R,M)_{\mathfrak m}\to D^i(A\mid R,M)_{\mathfrak m}\oplus D^i(A\mid R,M)_{\mathfrak m}\] is an isomorphism for every maximal ideal \(\mathfrak m\) of (A) and hence \[\Delta:D^i(A\mid R,M)\to D^i(A\mid R,M)\oplus D^i(A\mid R,M)\] is an isomorphism, which entails that (D^i(A\mid R,M)=0) for all (i).

(6) In view of 3.12(4), we may assume that (A) is an (R)-algebra of finite type. Now consider the map \[E\otimes_A\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M)\to\operatorname{Der}_R(-,E\otimes_AM)\] in (C_{A\mid R}^{\sim}). Since (E) is (A)-flat, we have an isomorphism \[E\otimes_A\operatorname{Der}_R(B,M)\xrightarrow{\sim}\operatorname{Der}_R(B,E\otimes_AM)\] whenever (B\in\operatorname{ob}(C_{A\mid R})) is of finite type over (R). It follows from 3.3(b) that \[D^i(A\mid R,E\otimes_A\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M))\to D^i(A\mid R,E\otimes_AM)\] is an isomorphism. On the other hand, the functor (E\otimes_A-:(A\text{-mod})\to(A\text{-mod})) is exact, and therefore we have \[E\otimes_A D^i(A\mid R,M)=D^i(A\mid R,E\otimes_A\operatorname{Der}_R(-,M)),\] and hence our assertion.

(7) follows from 3.3(a).

(8) In view of 3.10 and 3.11, it suffices to show that \[\operatorname{Coker}(\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(I/I^2,M))=\operatorname{Exalcomm}(A\mid R,M),\] where (operatorname{Exalcomm}(A\mid R,M)) denotes the set of isomorphism classes of (R)-algebra extensions of (A) by (M). Let (0\to M\to A'\to A\to0) be an (R)-algebra extension of (A) by (M). Since (P) is a polynomial algebra over (R), we obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix{0\ar[r]&I\ar[r]\ar[d]&P\ar[r]\ar[d]&A\ar[r]\ar@{=}[d]&0\\0\ar[r]&M\ar[r]&A'\ar[r]&A\ar[r]&0}\] where (I\to M) is uniquely determined by the extension (A') up to (operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)). We thus obtain a map \[\Phi:\operatorname{Exalcomm}(A\mid R,M)\to\operatorname{Coker}(\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(I/I^2,M)).\] Conversely a (P)-linear map (I\to M) yields an extension by push-out \[\xymatrix{0\ar[r]&I\ar[r]\ar[d]&P\ar[r]\ar[d]&A\ar[r]\ar@{=}[d]&0\\0\ar[r]&M\ar[r]&A'\ar[r]&A\ar[r]&0.}\] Two (P)-linear maps (I\rightrightarrows M) which differ by (operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)) is trivially seen to yield isomorphic extensions and therefore we obtain a map \[\Psi:\operatorname{Coker}(\operatorname{Der}_R(P,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(I/I^2,M))\to\operatorname{Exalcomm}(A\mid R,M).\] It is straightforward to verify that \(\Phi,\Psi\) are inverse process to each other.

Corollary 3.13.

(a) (R)-algebra (A) is formally smooth over (R) if and only if (D^1(A\mid R,M)=0) for every (A)-module (M).

(b) Let (R) be noetherian and (A) an (R)-algebra of finite type. Then (A) is a relative complete-intersection over (R) if and only if (D^2(A\mid R,M)=0) for every (A)-module (M).

(c) Let (R) be noetherian and (A) an (R)-algebra of finite type. Then for any multiplicative subset (S) in (A), we have a canonical isomorphism \[D^i(S^{-1}A\mid R,S^{-1}M)\simeq S^{-1}D^i(A\mid R,M)\quad\text{for all }i\geq0.\]

(d) Let (R) be noetherian and (A) a local (R)-algebra of essentially finite type. We denote by \(\widehat A\) the \(\mathfrak m\)-adic completion of (A) where \(\mathfrak m\) = the maximal ideal of (A). Then for any (A)-module (E) of finite type we have a canonical isomorphism \[D^i(A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\simeq\widehat A\otimes_A D^i(A\mid R,E).\]

Proof: (a) follows immediately from (D^1(A\mid R,M)=\operatorname{Exalcomm}(A\mid R,M)).

(b) Let (P) be a polynomial algebra over (R) in a finite number of variables and let (0\to I\to P\to A\to0) be exact. Localizing if necessary, we may assume that (I) is generated by a (P)-regular sequence which would imply that (H_1(K_I)=0) and therefore \[D^2(A\mid R,M)=\operatorname{Coker}(H^1(K_I,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(H_1(K_I),M))=0.\] Conversely assume that (D^2(A\mid R,M)=0) for every (A)-module (M).

Let (0\to I\to P\to A\to0) be exact where (P) is a polynomial algebra over (R) in a finite number of variables. We have \[0=D^2(A\mid R,M)=\operatorname{Coker}(H^1(K_I,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(H_1(K_I),M))\] i.e., (H^1(K_I,M)\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(H_1(K_I),M)) is surjective for all (A)-module (M). Choose an exact sequence (0\to K\to F\to I\to0) where (F) is a free (P)-module of finite type. We note that (H_1(K_I)=K/K_0) where (K_0=\operatorname{Im}(\bigwedge^2F\to F)). The fact that (D^2(A\mid R,H_1(K_I))=0) i.e., \[H^1(K_I,H_1(K_I))\to\operatorname{Hom}_A(H_1(K_I),H_1(K_I))\text{ is surjective}\] entails that (0\to K/K_0\to F/IF\to I/I^2\to0) is exact and splits as (A)-modules and in particular (I/I^2) is projective as (A)-module. Therefore, localizing (A) if necessary, we may assume that (I/I^2) is (A)-free, and that (F/IF\to I/I^2) is an isomorphism. Then the fact that (0\to K/K_0\to F/IF\to I/I^2\to0) is exact and splits as (A)-modules entails that (H_1(K_I)=K/K_0=0). This means that (I) is generated by a (P)-regular sequence i.e., (A) is a relative complete intersection over (R).

(c) follows immediately from 3.12(4) and (6).

(d) Firstly it follows from 3.12(6) that \[D^i(A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\simeq\widehat A\otimes_A D^i(A\mid R,E)\] is an isomorphism for all (i), and therefore it suffices to show that

\[D^1(\widehat A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\to D^1(A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\] is an isomorphism. Our proof is based on the description of (D^1) in terms of algebra extensions. Let \[0\to\widehat A\otimes_AE\to A'\xrightarrow{\varphi}A\to0\] be an (R)-algebra extension. The fact that (\widehat A\otimes_AE) is an (\widehat A)-module of finite type entails readily that (A') is a local (R)-algebra, complete with respect to (\mathfrak m')-adic topology where (\mathfrak m') = the maximal ideal of (A'). Therefore if there is an (R)-algebra map (\psi:A\to A') such that (\varphi\circ\psi=\operatorname{id}_A), then (\psi) extends uniquely to (\widehat\psi:\widehat A\to A') such that (\varphi\circ\widehat\psi=\operatorname{id}_{\widehat A}). This proves that \[D^1(\widehat A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\to D^1(A\mid R,\widehat A\otimes_AE)\] is injective. Now let \[0\to\widehat A\otimes_AE\to B\xrightarrow{\varphi}A\to0\] be an (R)-algebra extension. Firstly we claim that the topology on (\widehat E=\widehat A\otimes_AE) induced from (\mathfrak m_B)-adic topology on (B) coincides with the topology on (\widehat E) as (\widehat A)-module. Indeed, since (A) is essentially of finite type over (R), one can find a subalgebra (B_0) of (B) such that (B_0) is a local (R)-algebra of essentially finite type over (R) and that (\varphi(B_0)=A). Set (E_0=\widehat E\cap B_0) and denote by (\mathfrak m_B,\mathfrak m_0,\mathfrak m) the maximal ideals of (B,B_0,A) respectively. Then (\mathfrak m_B=\mathfrak m_0+\widehat E) and hence (\mathfrak m_B^{n+1}=\mathfrak m_0^{n+1}+\mathfrak m^n\widehat E) for all (n). Since (B_0) is noetherian, there exists an integer (r>0) by Artin-Rees Theorem such that \[ \begin{aligned} \widehat E\cap\mathfrak m_B^{n+1} &=\mathfrak m^n\widehat E+\widehat E\cap\mathfrak m_0^{n+1}\\ &=\mathfrak m^n\widehat E+\mathfrak m^{n+1-r}(E_0\cap\mathfrak m_0^r) \subset\mathfrak m^{n+1-r}\widehat E \end{aligned} \] for all (n\geq r), which proves our assertion about the two topologies on (E). In particular, (\widehat E=\widehat A\otimes_AE) is complete with respect to the topology induced from (\mathfrak m_B)-adic topology on (B), and hence we obtain the exact commutative diagram \[ \xymatrix{ 0\ar[r]&\widehat E\ar[r]\ar@{=}[d]&B\ar[r]\ar[d]&A\ar[r]\ar[d]&0\\ 0\ar[r]&\widehat E\ar[r]&\widehat B\ar[r]&\widehat A\ar[r]&0 } \] where (\widehat B) stands for (\mathfrak m_B)-adic completion of (B). This proves that (D^1(\widehat A\mid R,\widehat E)\to D^1(A\mid R,\widehat E)) is surjective and hence we are done.

Corollary 3.14.

Let (R) be noetherian and (A) an (R)-algebra of finite type, smooth everywhere except at one closed point (x\in\operatorname{Spec}(A)). We then have isomorphisms \[D^1(A\mid R,A)\simeq D^1(A_x\mid R,A_x)\simeq D^1(\widehat A_x\mid R,\widehat A_x).\]

Proof: Since (A) is smooth over (R) except at (x\in\operatorname{Spec}(A)), (D^1(A\mid R,A)) is an (A)-module of finite type with its support on the set \(\{x\}\), and therefore we have \[D^1(A\mid R,A)=D^1(A\mid R,A)_x=\widehat{D^1(A\mid R,A)}_x.\] Therefore our assertion follows from 3.13.

Remark 3.15. Let (R) be topological ring and (A) a topological (R)-algebra. We then define, for any (A)-module (E), \[D^i_{\mathrm{top}}(A\mid R,E)=\varprojlim_\alpha D^i(A_\alpha\mid R_\alpha,A_\alpha\otimes_AE)\] where (A_\alpha=A/J_\alpha), (R_\alpha=R/I_\alpha), and (J_\alpha,I_\alpha) are both open in (A,R) respectively. Then (A) is formally smooth topological (R)-algebra means that (D^1_{\mathrm{top}}(A\mid R,E)=0) for every (A)-module (E).

Now let (X) be a scheme over a ring (R) and (E) a quasi-coherent (X)-module. We then define the sheaves \[\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)=D^i(X\mid R,E)\] on (X) via presheaves on (X) given by \[[\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)](U)=D^i(\Gamma(U,\mathcal O_X)\mid R,\Gamma(U,E))\] for every affine open set (U\subset X). If (R) is noetherian and (X) is of finite type over (R), then the property 3.13(a) shows that (\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)) can actually be constructed canonically, as a quasi-coherent (X)-module.

In case when there is no possible confusion we shall abreviate by setting (\underline D^i_{X\mid R}=\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(\mathcal O_X)). All the statements of 3.13 can now be translated in terms of these quasi-coherent sheaves (\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)), which we leave to the readers. We list below a few properties which is most relevant to our purpose.

Corollary 3.15.

Let (R) be noetherian and (X) an (R)-scheme of finite type. Then

(a) For any coherent (X)-module (E), (\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)) is again a coherent (X)-module for all (i), and furthermore \[\operatorname{Supp}\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)\subset X^{\mathrm{Sing}}\quad\text{for all }i>0\] where (X^{\mathrm{Sing}}=\{x\in X\mid X\text{ is non-smooth over }R\text{ at the point }x\in X\}).

(c) If (X) is a relative complete intersection over (R), then for every surjective ring homomorphism (S\to R) we have the exact sequence \[0\to\underline D^1_{X\mid R}(E)\to\underline D^1_{X\mid S}(E) \to\underline{\operatorname{Hom}}_{\mathcal O_X}(I/I^2\otimes_R\mathcal O_X,E)\to0\] where (I=\operatorname{Ker}(S\to R)). If, furthermore, (R) is local with the residue field (k) and (X) is (R)-flat, then we have the exact sequence \[0\to\underline D^1_{X_0\mid k}(\mathcal O_{X_0})\to \underline D^1_{X\mid S}(\mathcal O_{X_0})\to D^1(R\mid S,k)\otimes_k\mathcal O_{X_0}\to0\] where (X_0=X\otimes_Rk).

Proof: (a) Follows from 3.12(6) and (8) whereas (b) follows from 3.12(3).

(c) Since (X) is a relative complete-intersection over (R), we have (\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)=0) for all (i\geq2) by 3.12(10) and hence \[0\to\underline D^0_{X\mid R}(E)\to\underline D^0_{X\mid S}(E) \to\underline D^0_{R\mid S}(E)\to\underline D^1_{X\mid R}(E) \to\underline D^1_{X\mid S}(E)\to\underline D^1_{R\mid S}(E)\to0\] is exact by 3.12(2), where (\underline D^i_{R\mid S}(E)) are quasi-coherent sheaves of (X) defined by \[[\underline D^i_{R\mid S}(E)](U)=D^i(R\mid S,E(U))\] for every affine open subset (U\subset X). If (X) is (R)-flat, we have \[\underline D^i_{X_0\mid k}(E)\simeq\underline D^i_{X\mid R}(E)\] for all (i) and for all quasi-coherent (X_0)-module (E) and in particular \[\underline D^1_{X_0\mid k}(\mathcal O_{X_0})\simeq \underline D^1_{X\mid R}(\mathcal O_{X_0})\] from which the second exact sequence follows.

4. Formal moduli of deformations

One amongst the nice cofibered categories in which the isomorphism classes of objects are usually not prorepresentable, but nevertheless admit a formal versal object is the cofibered category of deformations. We now come back to the situations and notations of paragraph 1. Thus, throughout the section, (\Lambda) is a fixed complete local noetherian ring with the residue field (k), and (C_\Lambda) is the category of artinian local (\Lambda)-algebras with the same residue field (k).

Let (X_0) be a fixed scheme over the field (k). By an (infinitesimal) deformation of (X_0), we mean a pair ((R,X)) where (R\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)) and (X) is a flat (R)-scheme together with a commutative diagram \[ \xymatrix{ X_0\ar[r]^{i_X}\ar[d]&X\ar[d]^{\mathrm{flat}}\\ \operatorname{Spec}(k)\ar[r]&\operatorname{Spec}(R) } \]

such that the induced morphism \(X_0\to X\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(k)\) is an isomorphism. We note that, since \(R\) is an artinian local ring, \(i_X:X_0\to X\) is necessarily a closed immersion which is a homeomorphism on underlying topological spaces. If \((R,X)\), \((R',X')\) are deformations of \(X_0\) to \(R,R'\) respectively, we define an arrow \((R',X')\to(R,X)\) to consist of a map \(R'\xrightarrow{\varphi}R\) in \(C_\Lambda\) and a morphism \(\Phi:X\to X'\) of schemes with a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{&X_0\ar[dl]_{i_X}\ar[dr]^{i_{X'}}\ar[d]&\\X\ar[rr]^{\Phi}\ar[dr]&\operatorname{Spec}(k)\ar[d]\ar[dl]\ar[dr]&X'\ar[dl]\\&\operatorname{Spec}(R)\ar[rr]_{\operatorname{Spec}(\varphi)}&\operatorname{Spec}(R')}\]

We thus obtain the category \(M_{X_0}\) of (infinitesimal) deformations of \(X_0\) in which the objects and the arrows are defined as above. The category \(M_{X_0}\) is provided with a functor \(p:M_{X_0}\to C_\Lambda\) defined by \((R,X)\mapsto R\). Now let \((R,X)\in\operatorname{ob}(M_{X_0})\) and a map \(R\xrightarrow{\varphi}S\) in \(C_\Lambda\) be given. We then obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=5em@R=3em{X\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(S)\ar[r]^{p_1}\ar[d]_{\mathrm{flat}}&X\ar[d]^{\mathrm{flat}}\\\operatorname{Spec}(S)\ar[r]_{\operatorname{Spec}(\varphi)}&\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\] and \(\varphi\)-arrow \((\varphi,p_1):(R,X)\to(S,X\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(S))\) is obviously cocartesian in \(M_{X_0}\). It follows that the category \(M_{X_0}\) is a cofibered category over \(C_\Lambda\) via \(P:M_{X_0}\to C_\Lambda\). We observe the following facts:

4.1(a): Let \((1_R,\Phi):(R,X_1)\to(R,X_2)\) be an arrow in \(M_{X_0}\) i.e., \(\Phi:X_2\to X_1\) is a morphism over \(R\) such that \(X_2\otimes_Rk\to X_1\otimes_Rk\) is an isomorphism. Since \(R\) is artinian and \(X_i\) is \(R\)-flat, it follows immediately that \(\Phi\) is an isomorphism so that \((1_R,\Phi):(R,X_1)\to(R,X_2)\) is an isomorphism in \(M_{X_0}\). Consequently, \(M_{X_0}\) is a cofibered category in groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\).

4.1(b): Suppose that \(X_0\) is affine, say \(X_0=\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)\). Then for any object \((R,X)\in\operatorname{ob}(M_{X_0})\), \(X\) is also affine. Consequently, for any \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)\), the category \(M_{X_0}(R)\) is canonically equivalent, via the sections, to the category in which an object is a flat algebra \(A\) over \(R\) together with an \(R\)-algebra map \(j_A:A\to A_0\) inducing an isomorphism \(A\otimes_Rk\xrightarrow{\sim}A_0\), and a map \(\Phi:A_1\to A_2\) is an \(R\)-algebra map with a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=5em@R=3em{A_1\ar[r]^{\Phi}\ar[d]_{j_{A_1}}&A_2\ar[d]^{j_{A_2}}\\A_0\ar@{=}[r]&A_0}\]

4.1(c): Consider the cofibered category \(\widehat M_{X_0}\) in groupoids over \(\widehat C_\Lambda\). By definition, the category \(\widehat M_{X_0}(R)\) for any \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(\widehat C_\Lambda)\) is canonically equivalent to the category of formal schemes \(\mathfrak X\), adic over the formal spectrum \(\operatorname{Spf}(R)\), such that \(X_n=\mathfrak X\otimes_RR/\mathfrak m^{n+1}\) is flat over \(R/\mathfrak m^{n+1}\) for every \(n\geq0\) (or what amounts to the same, if \(X_0\) is noetherian, such that \(\mathfrak X\) is flat over \(\operatorname{Spf}(R)\)). Consequently, if \(X_0\) is noetherian, \(\widehat M_{X_0}\) is canonically \(\widehat C_\Lambda\)-equivalent to the category of deformations of \(X_0\) over the objects in \(\widehat C_\Lambda\), in the sense of formal schemes.

4.1(d): Let \((R,X)\in\operatorname{ob}(M_{X_0})\) i.e., we have a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{X_0\ar[r]^{i_X}\ar[d]&X\ar[d]^{\mathrm{flat}}\\\operatorname{Spec}(k)\ar[r]&\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\] such that \(X_0\to X\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(k)\) is an isomorphism. Now let \(U_0\subset X_0\) be a fixed open subscheme of \(X_0\). Since \(i_X:X_0\to X\) is a closed immersion which is a homeomorphism on the underlying topological spaces, we obtain a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{U_0\ar[r]^{i_X|U_0}\ar[d]&X|U_0\ar[d]^{\mathrm{flat}}\\\operatorname{Spec}(k)\ar[r]&\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\] such that \(U_0\to(X|U_0)\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(k)=X\otimes_Rk|U_0\) is an isomorphism, so that \((R,X|U_0)\) is a deformation of \(U_0\) to \(R\). Furthermore, for any arrow \((\varphi,\Phi):(R,X)\to(R',X')\) in \(M_{X_0}\), \((\varphi,\Phi|U_0):(R,X|U_0)\to(R',X'|U_0)\) is an arrow in \(M_{U_0}\). Consequently we have the restriction functor \(M_{X_0}\to M_{U_0}\) over \(C_\Lambda\). One may also consider a localization as well as a formalization at a point. Namely let \(x_0\) be a fixed point in \(X_0\). If \((R,X)\in\operatorname{ob}(M_{X_0})\), then \(\operatorname{Spec}(\mathcal O_{X,x_0})\) is a deformation of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\mathcal O_{X_0,x_0})\) to \(R\) and \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X,x_0})\) is a deformation of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_0})\) over \(R\), where \(\widehat{\ }\) stands for the completion with respect to the linear topology given by the powers of the maximal ideal. We thus obtain functors \[M_{X_0}\to M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\mathcal O_{X_0,x_0})}\to M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_0})}.\]

Firstly we establish that \(M_{X_0}\) is a homogeneous cofibered category over \(C_\Lambda\).

Lemma 4.2. Consider a diagram
\[\xymatrix@C=5em@R=2em{R'\ar[d]&\\R\ar[r]_-{\mathrm{surjective}}&\bar R}\]

in \(C_\Lambda\), and let \(E,E'\) be flat modules over \(R,R'\) respectively. Then for any \(R'\)-homomorphism \(E'\to\bar E=E\otimes_R\bar R\), the fibre-product \(E\times_{\bar E}E'\) is flat as \(R\times_{\bar R}R'\)-module.

Proof: Let \(0\to I\to R\to\bar R\to0\) be exact so that \(0\to I\to R\times_{\bar R}R'\to R'\to0\) is exact. Since \(E\) is \(R\)-flat, \(0\to I\otimes_RE\to E\to\bar E\to0\) is exact and hence we obtain the exact sequence \[(*)\qquad0\to I\otimes_RE\to E\times_{\bar E}E'\to E'\to0.\] Set \(S=R\times_{\bar R}R'\). Since \(I\) is nilpotent, \(E\times_{\bar E}E'\) is flat as an \(S\)-module if and only if \((E\times_{\bar E}E')\otimes_SR'\) is flat as an \(R'\)-module and \(\operatorname{Tor}_1^S(E\times_{\bar E}E',R')=0\). However, we note that \(I\otimes_RE=I\otimes_S(E\times_{\bar E}E')\) and \(E'=R'\otimes_S(E\times_{\bar E}E')\) and therefore the exact sequence \((*)\) can be rewritten as \[0\to I\otimes_S(E\times_{\bar E}E')\to E\times_{\bar E}E'\to R'\otimes_S(E\times_{\bar E}E')\to0.\] This means that \(\operatorname{Tor}_1^S(R',E\times_{\bar E}E')=0\) and \(R'\otimes_S(E\times_{\bar E}E')=E'\) is flat as \(R'\)-module, and therefore \(E\times_{\bar E}E'\) is \(S\)-flat.

Corollary 4.3. Given a diagram
\[\xymatrix@C=7em@R=3em{(R,X)\ar[dr]_{(\varphi,\Phi)}&(R',X')\ar[dl]^{(\varphi',\Phi')}\\&(\bar R,\bar X)&}\]

in \(M_{X_0}\), the amalgamated sum \(X\coprod_{\bar X}X'\) (in the sense of schemes) is a flat scheme over \(R\times_{\bar R}R'\), provided \(\varphi:R\to\bar R\) is surjective. Consequently, \(M_{X_0}\) is a homogeneous cofibered category in groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\).

Proof: For each affine open \(U_0\subset X_0\), we have by definition that \[X\coprod_{\bar X}X'|U_0\simeq\operatorname{Spec}\bigl(\Gamma(U_0,X)\times_{\Gamma(U_0,\bar X)}\Gamma(U_0,X')\bigr).\] Consequently, if \(\varphi:R\to\bar R\) is surjective, \(X\coprod_{\bar X}X'\) is a flat scheme over \(R\times_{\bar R}R'\) by 4.2, and the morphism \(i_X\coprod_{i_{\bar X}}i_{X'}:X_0\to X\coprod_{\bar X}X'\) induces an isomorphism \(X_0\to(X\coprod_{\bar X}X')\otimes_Sk\), where \(S=R\times_{\bar R}R'\). Therefore \((R\times_{\bar R}R',X\coprod_{\bar X}X')\in\operatorname{ob}(M_{X_0})\) and we obviously have that \[(R\times_{\bar R}R',X\coprod_{\bar X}X')=(R,X)\times_{(\bar R,\bar X)}(R',X')\] in the category \(M_{X_0}\), which means that \(M_{X_0}\) is homogeneous over \(C_\Lambda\).

Lemma 4.4. For any scheme \(X_0\) over \(k\), we have a canonical exact sequence
\[0\to H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\to H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\]

where \(D^i_{X_0}\) are as defined in paragraph 3.

Proof: (Special case) Assume that \(X_0\) is affine and let \(\Gamma(X_0,\mathcal O_{X_0})=A_0\). Then as it was noted in 4.1(b), the category \(M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])\) is canonically equivalent to the category \(E\), where

\(\operatorname{ob}(E)\): object in \(E\) is a flat algebra \(A\) over \(k[\epsilon]\) together with a \(k[\epsilon]\)-algebra map \(j_A:A\to A_0\) inducing the isomorphism \(k\otimes_{k[\epsilon]}j_A:k\otimes_{k[\epsilon]}A\xrightarrow{\sim}A_0\). Alternatively, an object in \(E\) is a \(k\)-algebra \(A\) together with an exact sequence \(0\to A_0\xrightarrow{\epsilon}A\xrightarrow{j_A}A_0\to0\) in which \(\epsilon(A_0)^2=0\) as an ideal in \(A\).

\(\operatorname{Fl}(E)\): An arrow \(A_1\to A_2\) in \(E\) with respect to first description of the object in \(E\) is a \(k[\epsilon]\)-algebra map \(\Phi:A_1\to A_2\) such that \(j_{A_1}=j_{A_2}\circ\Phi\). Therefore, an arrow \(A_1\to A_2\) in \(E\) with respect to the alternative description of objects in \(E\) is a \(k\)-algebra map \(\Phi:A_1\to A_2\) yielding a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{0\ar[r]&A_0\ar[r]^{\epsilon}\ar@{=}[d]&A_1\ar[r]^{j_{A_1}}\ar[d]^{\Phi}&A_0\ar[r]\ar@{=}[d]&0\\0\ar[r]&A_0\ar[r]&A_2\ar[r]^{j_{A_2}}&A_0\ar[r]&0.}\] It follows that \([M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\) is canonically isomorphic, via \(\Gamma(X_0,-)\), to the set of isomorphism classes of \(k\)-algebra extensions of \(A_0\) by \(A_0\). In other words, we have a canonical bijection \([M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\xrightarrow{\sim}H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\) via \(\Gamma(X_0,-)\), which is trivially seen to respect the vector space structures on both sides.

(General case) For each affine open \(U_0\subset X_0\), we get a canonical map \[[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\to H^0(U_0,D^1_{X_0})\] defined as the composite map \[[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\to[M_{U_0}(k[\epsilon])]\xrightarrow{\sim}H^0(U_0,D^1_{X_0})\] and hence we obtain a canonical map \[[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\to H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0}).\] Its kernel is the set of isomorphism classes of locally-trivial deformations of \(X_0\) to \(k[\epsilon]\), i.e., the set of isomorphism classes of deformations of \(X_0\) to \(k[\epsilon]\), locally (on \(X\)) isomorphic to \(X_0\otimes_k k[\epsilon]\). Since \(D^0_{X_0}=\operatorname{Der}_k(\mathcal O_{X_0},\mathcal O_{X_0})= \operatorname{Aut}(X_0\otimes_k k[\epsilon]|X_0)\) = the sheaf of germs of automorphisms of \(X_0\otimes_k k[\epsilon]\) inducing the identity on the subscheme \(X_0\), it is canonically isomorphic to \(H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\), so that we obtain the desired exact sequence.

Theorem 4.5.

However this does not imply that non-smooth points of \(X_0\) are all isolated. There are affine schemes \(X_0\) over \(k\) having a non-isolated non-smooth point but \(\operatorname{supp}D^1_{X_0}\) is a finite set.

Remark 4.6(b): Henceforth a deformation of \(X_0\) is meant by an object in \(M_{X_0}\). In case when there is a possible confusion, an object in \(M_{X_0}\) will be referred as an infinitesimal deformation of \(X_0\).

Remark 4.6(c): Let \(X_0\) be a scheme over \(k\). We have fixed a complete local noetherian ring \(\Lambda\) with the residue field \(k\), and considered the deformations of \(X_0\) over the category \(C_\Lambda\). (In practice one usually takes \(\Lambda=k\) or \(W(k)\) if \(k\) is a perfect field of positive characteristic.) Now \(C_k\subset C_\Lambda\) is a fully faithful imbedding, and that \(M_{X_0}|C_k\) is simply the category of deformations of \(X_0\) over \(S\) where \(S\in\operatorname{ob}(C_k)\). Therefore if \((R,\mathfrak X)\) is a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\) over \(C_\Lambda\), then \((k\otimes_\Lambda R,k\otimes_\Lambda\mathfrak X)\) is a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\) over \(C_k\). It follows, for example, that \(\dim_{C_\Lambda}M_{X_0}=\dim k\otimes_\Lambda R\) does not depend on the choice of \(\Lambda\) but depends only on \(X_0\).

One can generalize the above theorem in many ways. Let us for instance consider a pair \(Y_0\to X_0\) where \(X_0\) is a scheme of finite type over \(k\) and \(Y_0\) a fixed closed subscheme of \(X_0\). For each \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(C_\Lambda)\), a deformation of \((X_0,Y_0)\) to \(R\) is meant to be a commutative diagram

\[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{Y_0\ar[r]\ar[dr]\ar[d]&Y\ar[dr]^{\mathrm{flat}}\ar[d]&\\ X_0\ar[r]\ar[d]&X\ar[d]^{\mathrm{flat}}\\ \operatorname{Spec}(k)\ar[r]&\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\]

such that \((X_0,Y_0)\to(X\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(k), Y\times_{\operatorname{Spec}(R)}\operatorname{Spec}(k))\) is an isomorphism. Defining a morphism \((R',X',Y')\to(R,X,Y)\) of deformations of \((X_0,Y_0)\) to be the obvious ones, we obtain the cofibered category \(M_{X_0,Y_0}\) in groupoids over \(C_\Lambda\). (This is a process often used in rigidification of automorphisms in moduli problems of deformations.) Proof: We can complete \(U_0^1,\ldots,U_0^r\) into an affine open covering of \(X_0\) by choosing \(U_0^{r+1},\ldots,U_0^N\) such that \(U_0^j\cap X_0^{\mathrm{Sing}}=\varnothing\) for all \(j>r\). Now let \(R'\to R\) be a small extension in \(C_\Lambda\), and let \((R',U'{}^1),\ldots,(R',U'{}^r)\) be deformations of \(U_0^1,\ldots,U_0^r\) to \(R'\) such that \(U'{}^i\otimes_{R'}R=X|U_0^i\), \(1\leq i\leq r\), for some deformation \((R,X)\) of \(X_0\) to \(R\). We must show that there exists a deformation \((R',X')\) of \(X_0\) to \(R'\) such that \(X'\otimes_{R'}R=X\) and \(X'|U_0^i=U'{}^i\) for \(1\leq i\leq r\). Since \(U_0^j\) for \(j>r\) is smooth over \(k\), \(X|U_0^j\) can be lifted, by 4.9(a), to a deformation \((R',U'{}^j)\), i.e., there exist deformations \((R',U'{}^j)\) of \(U_0^j\) to \(R'\) such that \(U'{}^j\otimes_{R'}R=X|U_0^j\) for all \(j>r\). Since, for any pair \(i\neq j\), \(U_0^i\cap U_0^j\) is affine and smooth over \(k\), we obtain an isomorphism

\[\tau_{i,j}:U'{}^i|U_0^i\cap U_0^j\xrightarrow\sim U'{}^j|U_0^i\cap U_0^j\]

such that \(\tau_{i,j}\otimes_{R'}R=\operatorname{id}_{X|U_0^i\cap U_0^j}\), and in turn defines a cohomology class in \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\) given by

\[U_0^i\cap U_0^j\cap U_0^k\longmapsto \tau_{i,j}\tau_{j,k}\tau_{i,k}^{-1}.\]

Since \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\) by hypothesis, the obstruction for glueing these to obtain a global deformation vanishes, and hence we get a deformation \((R',X')\) of \(X_0\) to \(R'\) such that \(X'\otimes_{R'}R=X\) and \(X'|U_0^j\simeq U'{}^j\). This completes our proof.

Remark 4.12. The previous proof assumes \(X_0\) to be separated, which is unnecessary. In fact, for a fixed deformation \((R,X)\) of \(X_0\) and deformations \((R',U'{}^j)\) of \(U_0^j\), the deformations \((R',V')\) of open set \(V\) of \(X\) to \(R'\) compatible with the deformation \((R',U'{}^j)\) already given, form a “gerbe” (in Giraud's terminology) whose structure sheaf is \(D^0_{X_0}\). Hence the obstruction to the existence of a section of this “gerbe” (i.e., a global deformation \((R',X')\) of \(X\) compatible with the given datum \((R',U'{}^j)\)) is in \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\) which is zero by assumption.

Corollary 4.13.

Let \(X_0\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type, smooth outside a finite subset \(X_0^{\mathrm{Sing}}=\{x_1,\ldots,x_r\}\) of \(X_0\) such that \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\). Then

(a) \(M_{X_0}\to M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1})} \times\cdots\times M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})}\) is smooth. In particular,

\[\underline{\dim}\,M_{X_0}=\dim H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})+ \sum_{i=1}^r\underline{\dim}\,M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_i})}.\]

(b) If furthermore \(X_0\) is locally a complete-intersection, then \(M_{X_0}\) is smooth.

Proof:

(a) The first statement follows from 4.10 and 4.9(b). Now let \((R,\mathcal X)\) and \((R_1,\mathcal X_1),\ldots,(R_r,\mathcal X_r)\) be formal versal deformations of \(X_0\) and \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1}),\ldots, \operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})\) respectively. We then obtain a commutative diagram

\[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{ h_R\ar[r]\ar[d]&h_{R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r}\ar[d]\\ M_{X_0}\ar[r]&M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1})}\times\cdots\times M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})}} \]

Since the composite functor

\[h_R\to M_{X_0}\to M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1})} \times\cdots\times M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})}\]

is smooth whereas

\[h_{R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r}\to M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1})}\times\cdots\times M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})}\]

is minimally smooth, it follows from 2.7(a) that

\[h_R\to h_{R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r}\]

is smooth i.e., \(R\) is a formal power-series ring over \(R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r\). Since

\[0\to H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\to [M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_1})}(k[\epsilon])] \cdots[M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_r})}(k[\epsilon])]\]

is exact by 4.4 i.e.,

\[0\to H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,k[\epsilon]) \to\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}} (R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r,k[\epsilon])\]

is exact, it follows that \(R\) is a formal power-series ring over \(R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r\) in \(d\)-variables where \(d=\dim_k H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\). Therefore

\[\begin{aligned} \underline{\dim}\,M_{X_0}=\dim k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R &=d+\dim k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda (R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r)\\ &=d+\dim((k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_1)\widehat\otimes_k\cdots \widehat\otimes_k(k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_r))\\ &=d+\sum_{i=1}^r\dim k\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_i =d+\sum_{i=1}^r\underline{\dim}\, M_{\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x_i})}. \end{aligned}\]

(b) follows from 4.10 and 4.9(a).

4.14. We close this paragraph with an explicit construction of a formal versal deformation in a simple situation. In fact, if \(X_0\) is affine and is a complete- intersection, it is trivial to write down explicitly. Thus let

\[A_0=k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)=S/J\]

where \(S=k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\), \(J=(f_1,\ldots,f_r)\), and \(f_1,\ldots,f_r\) is an \(S\)-regular sequence. We then obtain the exact sequence

\[\cdots\to\operatorname{Der}_k(S,A_0)\to \operatorname{Hom}_{A_0}(J/J^2,A_0)\to D^1(A_0|k,A_0)\to0.\]

We note that \(J/J^2\) is \(A_0\)-free module of rank \(r\) generated by \(f_1\pmod {J^2},\ldots,f_r\pmod {J^2}\), since \(f_1,\ldots,f_r\) is an \(S\)-regular sequence. We now assume that \(D^1(A_0|k,A_0)\) is a finite-dimensional vector space over \(k\), say \(A\) has isolated non-smooth points only. Let \(d=\dim_kD^1(A_0|k,A_0)\) and choose

\[p_j:J/J^2\to A_0\qquad(j=1,2,\ldots,d)\]

which form a \(k\)-basis of \(D^1(A_0|k,A_0)\). Now choose \(P_{ji}\in\Lambda[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) such that \(p_j(f_i)=P_{ji}\pmod {\mathfrak m_\Lambda+J}\) where \(\mathfrak m_\Lambda\) is the maximal ideal of \(\Lambda\). We then consider

\[R=\Lambda[[t_1,\ldots,t_d]],\qquad B=R[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(G_1,\ldots,G_r)\]

where \(G_j=F_j+\sum_{i=1}^d t_iP_{ji}\) and \(F_j\) is a polynomial with coefficients in \(\Lambda\) gotten from \(f_j\) by lifting the coefficients from \(k\) to \(\Lambda\). Since \(R[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) is \(R\)-flat and \(G_1,\ldots,G_r\) modulo \(\mathfrak m_R\) (which becomes \(f_1,\ldots,f_r\)) is a regular sequence in \(k\otimes_\Lambda R[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\), it follows that \(B\) is \(R\)-flat, and \(k\otimes_RB=A_0\). Furthermore,

\[\operatorname{Hom}_{\Lambda\text{-alg}}(R,k[\epsilon])\to D^1(A_0|k,A_0) \simeq[M_{\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)}(k[\epsilon])]\]

is bijective by our construction of \(B\). Therefore, if we set

\[\operatorname{Spf}(B)=\varprojlim_v\operatorname{Spec}(R/\mathfrak m^{v+1}\otimes_RB)\]

then \((R,\operatorname{Spf}(B))\) is an object in \(\widehat M_{\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)}\) and the functor \(h_R\to\widehat M_{\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)}\) induced by \((R,\operatorname{Spf}(B))\) is smooth by 1.6, since \(R\) is a formal power-series ring over \(\Lambda\). In other words, \((R,\operatorname{Spf}(B))\) is a formal versal object of \(M_{\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)}\).

Remark 4.15. Let \(X_0\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type with isolated non-smooth points only, and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\). The above construction shows that if \(X_0\) is affine and locally a complete-intersection, then \(\mathcal X\) is a formalization of an \(R\)-scheme of finite type. This fact can easily be generalized to the case when \(X_0\) is not necessarily affine but \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\).

Remark 4.16. The global theory of the relative cotangent complex (the latter theory being reduced to the truncated complex of length 1, which is sufficient however for many purposes of deformation theory) allows to generalize and simplify certain results, such as 4.10. Namely let \(S\) be a scheme, \(S_0\subset S'\subset S\) two subschemes of \(S\) defined by ideals \(J\supset I\) such that \(JI=0\), \(X'\) a scheme flat over \(S'\), \(L=L^{X_0/S_0}_{X'/S'}\) the relative cotangent complex of \(X_0\) over \(S_0\), which is an element of the derived category \(D(X_0,\mathcal O_{X_0})\). We consider the category \(F\) of all flat schemes \(X\) over \(S\) which extend \(X'/S'\), i.e., together with an \(S'\)-isomorphism \(X\times_SS'\simeq X'\). Then

  1. (a) For an object \(X'\) of \(F\), the group of automorphisms is canonically isomorphic to \(\operatorname{Ext}^0(X_0;L,I_{X_0})\) (where \(I_{X_0}\) is the inverse image of \(I\), viewed as a module on \(S_0\), on \(X_0\)).
  2. (b) The set of isomorphism classes of objects of \(F\) is empty or a torsor (= a principal homogeneous space) under the group \(\operatorname{Ext}^1(X_0;L,I_{X_0})\).
  3. (c) One can define a canonical element \(c\in\operatorname{Ext}^2(X_0;L,I_{X_0})\) whose vanishing is necessary and sufficient for \(F\) to be non empty.

In order to study the \(\operatorname{Ext}^i(X_0;L,I_{X_0})\), it is often convenient to write

\[\operatorname{Ext}^i(X_0;L,I_{X_0})= H^i(X_0,R\!\operatorname{Hom}(L,I_{X_0}))\]

and to notice that the complex \(R\!\operatorname{Hom}(L,I_{X_0})\) has as cohomology sheaves the sheaves \(D^i_{X_0}(I_{X_0})\) introduced in §3. For \(i=0,1,2\), these sheaves may be viewed respectively as the sheaf of automorphisms of any object \(X\) of \(F\) (understood: automorphisms inducing the identity on \(X'\)), the sheaf of indeterminacy of isomorphism classes of local solutions to the flat deformation problem, and the sheaf of obstructions to the existence of local solutions. On the other hand, the standard spectral sequence gives us

\[\operatorname{Ext}^{p+q}(X_0,L,I_{X_0})\Leftarrow H^p(X_0,D^q_{X_0}(I_{X_0})),\]

which yields the well-known isomorphism

\[\operatorname{Aut}(X/X')\simeq H^0(X_0,D^0_{X_0}(I_{X_0})) \simeq H^0(X_0,\mathcal Hom(\Omega^1_{X_0/S_0},I_{X_0})),\]

and the exact sequence in low degrees

\[0\to H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to\operatorname{Ext}^1\to H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\to H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to\operatorname{Ext}^2.\]

This shows in particular that if \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\), and if there exists any local flat deformation \(X\) of \(X'/S'\), then there exists a flat global deformation which corresponds to given local deformations. More precisely and generally, for a given local deformations (i.e. a section of the “sheaf of isomorphism classes of local flat deformations”), the argument 4.10 shows that the obstruction to finding a global flat deformation compatible with these is in \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\), hence such a global flat deformation exists if \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\): this is essentially 4.10.

On the other hand, the spectral sequence shows that the \(\operatorname{Ext}^2\) is zero whenever we have the relations

\[(*)\qquad H^0(X_0,D^2_{X_0})=0,\quad H^1(X_0,D^1_{X_0})=0,\quad H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0.\]

The first relation is always satisfied if \(X_0\) is a relative complete intersection over \(S_0\) (as then \(D^2_{X_0}=0\) for any module on \(X_0\)), the second is satisfied provided the support of \(D^1_{X_0}\) is discrete, as is the case if \(X_0\) is smooth over \(S_0\) except on a discrete subset of \(X_0\) over \(S_0\). When the previous three relations (*) are all satisfied, then by (c) above, \(F\) is non empty, which generalizes 4.12(b).

More geometrically, and without using the global theory of the relative cotangent complex (but only the globalizing of the functors \(D^i_{X_0}\)), one can get a simple geometric interpretation of the three successive obstructions to deforming flatly \(X'\) to \(X\), lying in the three left hand terms of (*). Namely by the local theory, the first obstruction \(c_1\) in \(H^0(X_0,D^2_{X_0})\) can be defined as the obstruction to finding local solutions, when this obstruction vanishes, the sheaf \(F\) of isomorphism classes of local solutions of the problem is (by the local theory) in a canonical way a torsor under \(D^1_{X_0}\), and the second obstruction \(c_2\), lying in \(H^1(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\), is the obstruction to finding a section of this torsor, i.e. of finding a “coherent system of isomorphism classes of local solutions”; if this second obstruction also vanishes, then, choosing arbitrarily a section of \(F\), one gets, as noted above, an obstruction \(c_3\) in \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\) to finding a global solution corresponding to the given system of (isomorphism classes of) local solutions. Using the fact that the section of \(F\) is determined up to adding a section of \(D^1_{X_0}\), we get a map \(d:H^1(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\to H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\), and a solution to the deformation problem exists if and only if \(c_1=0\), \(c_2=0\), and \(c_3\) is zero as an element of \(\operatorname{Coker}d\) (each \(c_i\) being defined when the previous ones are zero). Of course, the groups \(H^0(X_0,D^2_{X_0})\), \(H^1(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\), and \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})/\operatorname{Im}d\) are just the initial terms \(E_2^{0,2}\), \(E_2^{1,1}\), and \(E_3^{2,0}\) of the spectral sequence above, \(d\) being the differential (up to sign ?)

\[d_2:E_2^{0,1}\to E_2^{2,0}.\]

5. Formal Jacobian subschemes

Let \(X_0\) be a scheme of finite type over a field \(k\), and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\). Then \(\mathcal X\) is not an ordinary scheme over \(R\) but an \(R\)-adic formal scheme. The purpose of this section is to clarify “the non-smooth locus of \(\mathcal X\) over \(R\)”.

We firstly recall the definition of Fitting ideals associated to a module of finite type: Let \(A\) be a commutative ring and \(E\) an \(A\)-module of finite type. Choose a presentation

\[K\to F\to E\to0\]

where \(F\) is locally-free of constant rank \(n\). We define

\[I_A^p(E)=\operatorname{Im}(\wedge^{n-p}K\otimes\wedge^{n-p}F^*\to A) \qquad\text{for }p=0,1,2,\ldots\]

The ideals \(I_A^p(E)\) thus defined does not depend on the choice of a presentation and therefore are invariants of \(A\)-module \(E\), called the Fitting ideals of \(A\)-module \(E\). The following properties readily follow from the definition.

5.1. (a) For any ring homomorphism \(A\to B\), the canonical map

\[B\otimes_A A/I_A^p(E)\to B/I_B^p(B\otimes_AE)\]

is an isomorphism for all \(p\). In particular we have

\[I_{S^{-1}A}^p(S^{-1}E)=S^{-1}I_A^p(E)\]

for any multiplicative subset \(S\) in \(A\).

5.1. (b) We have \[I_A^0(E)\subset I_A^1(E)\subset I_A^2(E)\subset\cdots,\] and, for each point \(x\in\operatorname{Spec}(A)\), \[I_A^p(E)_x=A_x\quad\text{for all }p\geq\dim_{\kappa(x)}E(x),\] where \(E(x)=\kappa(x)\otimes_AE\).

(c) \(x\in\operatorname{Spec}(A)-\operatorname{Supp}A/I_A^p(E)\) if and only if, for any (or for some) presentation \[K\to F\to E\to0\] where \(F\simeq A^n\), \(K_x\) contains \(A_x^{n-p}\) which is isomorphic to a direct summand of \(F_x\). In particular, we have that \[\operatorname{Supp}A/I_A^0(E)=\operatorname{Supp}E.\]

(d) \[I_A^r(E_1\oplus E_2)=\sum_{p+q=r}I_A^p(E_1)\cdot I_A^q(E_2).\]

(e) If \(A\) is a noetherian adic ring, then \[A/I_A^p(E)=\varprojlim_\nu A_\nu/I_{A_\nu}^p(E_\nu)\] where \(A=\varprojlim_\nu A_\nu\) and \(E_\nu=E\otimes_AA_\nu\).

In view of the property 5.1(a), our definition of Fitting ideals is readily globalized. Thus let \(X\) be any scheme, and \(E\) a quasi-coherent \(X\)-module of finite presentation. We then obtain a sequence of quasi-coherent ideal sheaves \(I_X^p(E)\) for \(p\geq0\) defined by \[\Gamma(U,I_X^p(E))\simeq I_{\Gamma(U,\mathcal O_X)}^p(\Gamma(U,E))\] for every affine open subset \(U\subset X\).

Now let \(X\to Y\) be a morphism of schemes. The relative Kähler-differentials \(\Omega_{X|Y}(=\Omega^1_{X|Y})\) is a quasi-coherent \(X\)-module. If the morphism \(X\to Y\) is of essentially finite presentation, then \(\Omega_{X|Y}\) is a quasi-coherent \(X\)-module of finite presentation, and in turn we may consider the ideal sheaves \(I_X^p(\Omega_{X|Y})\).

Definition 5.2.

Let \(X\to Y\) be a morphism of essentially finite presentation. For each integer \(p\geq0\), we define \(J_X^p(X|Y)\) to be the closed subscheme of \(X\) defined by the ideal sheaf \[I_X^p(X|Y)=I_X^p(\Omega_{X|Y}),\] called the Jacobian subschemes of \(X\) over \(Y\). For each point \(x\in X\), we set \(X_x=\operatorname{Spec}(\mathcal O_{X,x})\).

Theorem 5.3.

Let \(X\to Y\) be a morphism of essentially finite presentation. Then

  1. (1) For any base change \(Y\leftarrow Y'\), the canonical map \[J_{X'}^p(X'|Y')\simeq J_X^p(X|Y)\times_YY'\] is an isomorphism for all \(p\), where \(X'=X\times_YY'\).
  2. (2) For each point \(x\in X\), we have the canonical isomorphism \[J_{X_x}^p(X_x|Y)\simeq J_X^p(X|Y)_x.\]
  3. (3) \(J_X^0(X|Y)\supset J_X^1(X|Y)\supset J_X^2(X|Y)\supset\cdots\), and \(x\notin J_X^p(X|Y)\) for all \[p\geq\dim_{\kappa(x)}\Omega_{X|Y}^1(x).\]
  4. (4) \(x\notin J_X^p(X|Y)\) if and only if there exists an open neighbourhood \(U\) of \(x\) which admits a closed immersion into a \(Y\)-scheme \(U'\) such that \(U'\) is smooth over \(Y\) at \(x'\) and \(\dim_xU'_{f(x)}=p\), where \(x'\) is the image of \(x\) under \(U\to U'\).
  5. (5) \[I_{X_1\times_YX_2}^r(X_1\times_YX_2|Y)= \sum_{p+q=r}\pi_1^*I_{X_1}^p(X_1|Y)\cdot\pi_2^*I_{X_2}^q(X_2|Y),\] where \(\pi_i:X_1\times_YX_2\to X_i\) is the projection.

Proof: (3) follows from 5.1(b) whereas (1) follows from 5.1(a) together with the fact that \(p^*\Omega_{X|Y}\simeq\Omega_{X'|Y'}\) is an isomorphism where \(p:X\times_YY'\to X\) is the projection. (2) also follows from 5.1(a) since \(\mathcal O_{X,x}\otimes_{\mathcal O_X}\Omega_{X|Y}\simeq\Omega_{X_x|Y}\) is an isomorphism. (5) follows from 5.1(d) and the isomorphism \[\pi_1^*\Omega_{X_1|Y}\oplus\pi_2^*\Omega_{X_2|Y} \simeq\Omega_{X_1\times_YX_2|Y}.\]

(4) Assume the existence of a diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{U\ar@{^{(}->}[r]^i\ar[d]_f&U'\ar[dl]^{f'}\\Y}\] where \(U'\) is smooth over \(Y\) at \(x'=i(x)\) and \(\dim_xU'_{f(x)}=p\). Then \(\dim_{\kappa(x')}\Omega_{U'|Y}(x')=p\) and \[i^*\Omega_{U'|Y}\to\Omega_{U|Y}\to0\] is exact, so that \(\dim_{\kappa(x)}\Omega_{U|Y}(x)\leq p\), which implies that \(x\notin J_U^p(U|Y)\) by 5.3(3). Since \(J_U^p(U|Y)=J_X^p(X|Y)|_U\), this means that \(x\notin J_X^p(X|Y)\). Now assume conversely that \(x\notin J_X^p(X|Y)\).

The question being local, we may assume that \(X,Y\) are both affine, say \(X=\operatorname{Spec}(B)\), \(Y=\operatorname{Spec}(A)\). Choose a presentation over \(A\) \[0\to J\to P\to B\to0\] where \(P=A[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\). Then \[J/J^2\xrightarrow d B\otimes_P\Omega_{P|A}\to\Omega_{B|A}\to0\] is exact and \(B\otimes_P\Omega_{P|A}\) is \(B\)-free of rank \(n\), and therefore \[I_B^p(\Omega_{B|A})=\operatorname{Im} (\wedge^{n-p}J/J^2\otimes_B(B\otimes_P\wedge^{n-p}\Omega_{P|A})^*\to B).\] Therefore, \(x\notin J_X^p(X|Y)\) if and only if there exist \(f_1,\ldots,f_{n-p}\) in \(J\) such that \[\left[\det\left(\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right)\right](x)\ne0\] for some choice of \(n-p\) variables among \(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n\); there exist \(f_1,f_2,\ldots,f_{n-p}\) in \(J\) such that \(\operatorname{Spec}(P/(f_1,\ldots,f_{n-p}))\to\operatorname{Spec}(A)\) is smooth at \(x'\), where \(x'\) is the image of \(x\) under \(\operatorname{Spec}(B)\to\operatorname{Spec}(P/(f_1,\ldots,f_{n-p}))\). Thus it suffices to take \(U'=\operatorname{Spec}(P/(f_1,\ldots,f_{n-p}))\).

The following is a generalization of the usual Jacobian criteria for relative situation.

Theorem 5.4.

Let \(X\xrightarrow{f}Y\) be a morphism of essentially finite presentation. Assume that there exists a fixed integer \(n\geq0\) with either conditions:

  1. (1) \(f\) is flat and \(\dim_xX_{f(x)}=n\) for every point \(x\in X\).
  2. (2) \(f\) is a relative complete-intersection with virtual dimension \(n\) (SGA6 VIII 1.9).

Then \(x\notin J_X^n(X|Y)\) if and only if \(f\) is smooth at \(x\).

Proof: We have, by 5.3(4) that \(x\notin J_X^n(X|Y)\) if and only if there exists a diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{U\ar@{^{(}->}[r]^i\ar[d]_f&U'\ar[dl]^{f'}\\Y}\] such that \(f'\) is smooth at \(x'\) and \(\dim_xU'_{f(x)}=n\), where \(i\) is a closed immersion and \(x'=i(x)\). Thus it suffices to show that either conditions (1) or (2) entails the isomorphism of \(i\) at the point \(x\). Set \(A=\mathcal O_{Y,f(x)}\), \(B'=\mathcal O_{U',x'}\), \(B=\mathcal O_{U,x}\) and let \[0\to I\to B'\to B\to0\] be exact. We must show that \(I=0\). Assume (1). Since \(B\) is \(A\)-flat, \[0\to I/\mathfrak mI\to B'/\mathfrak mB'\to B/\mathfrak mB\to0\] is exact where \(\mathfrak m\) is the maximal ideal of \(A\). By hypothesis, \(\dim_xU_{f(x)}=\dim_{x'}U'_{f(x)}\) so that \(\dim B'/\mathfrak mB'=\dim B/\mathfrak mB\). However \(B'/\mathfrak mB'\) is a regular local ring and therefore it entails that \(I/\mathfrak mI=0\) so that \(I=0\). Now assume (2), and consider the exact sequence \[I/I^2\xrightarrow dB\otimes_{B'}\Omega_{B'|A}\to\Omega_{B|A}\to0.\] Since \(X\) is a relative complete-intersection over \(Y\) and \(U'\to Y\) is smooth at \(x\), it follows that \(d:I/I^2\to B\otimes_{B'}\Omega_{B'|A}\) is injective at \(x\) and \(I/I^2\) and \(B\otimes_{B'}\Omega_{B'|A}\) are both free at \(x\). Therefore by the definition of virtual dimension of relative complete-intersection we must have that \[n=\dim_{\kappa(x)}\Omega_{B'|A}(x')-\dim_{\kappa(x)}I/I^2(x) =n-\dim_{\kappa(x)}I/I^2(x),\] i.e., \(I/I^2=0\), i.e., \(I=0\).

Definition 5.5.

Let \(X\xrightarrow{f}Y\) be a morphism of essentially finite presentation. In case when there exists a fixed integer \(n\) with the property (1) or (2) in 5.4, we set \[I_X(X|Y)=I_X^n(X|Y)\qquad\text{and}\qquad J_X(X|Y)=J_X^n(X|Y).\]

We can extend this notation to formal schemes in an obvious manner.

Thus let \(\mathcal X\) be a noetherian formal scheme and \(E\) a coherent \(\mathcal X\)-module. Then we can define the ideal sheaf \(I_{\mathcal X}^p(E)\) by setting \[I_{\mathcal X}^p(E)(U)=I_{\mathcal X(U)}^p(E(U))\] for every affine open \(U\subset\mathcal X\). If \(\mathcal X=\varprojlim_\nu X_\nu\), then \(E=\varprojlim_\nu E_\nu\), where \(E_\nu=E\otimes_{\mathcal O_\mathcal X}\mathcal O_{X_\nu}\), and it follows from the fact that \(\mathcal X\) is noetherian that \[I_{\mathcal X}^p(E)=\varprojlim_\nu I_{X_\nu}^p(E_\nu).\]

Now let \(\mathcal S\) be a noetherian formal scheme and \(\mathcal X\) an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type. Let \(I_\mathcal X\) be the ideal sheaf defining the closed immersion \(\Delta:\mathcal X\to\mathcal X\times_\mathcal S\mathcal X\) (= the fibre-product in the category of formal schemes), i.e., \[0\to I_\mathcal X\to\mathcal O_{\mathcal X\times_\mathcal S\mathcal X} \to\mathcal O_\mathcal X\to0\] is exact. We define \(\Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}=I_\mathcal X/I_\mathcal X^2\). Since \(\mathcal X\) is an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type, it follows that \(\mathcal X=\varprojlim_\nu X_\nu\), where \(X_\nu=\mathcal X\times_\mathcal S S_\nu\), and hence \[\Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}\simeq\varprojlim_\nu\Omega_{X_\nu|S_\nu}\] is a coherent \(\mathcal X\)-module. We note that for any formal \(\mathcal S\)-scheme \(\mathcal S'\) of finite type, we have a canonical isomorphism \[\Omega_{\mathcal X\times_\mathcal S\mathcal S'|\mathcal S'} \simeq\mathcal O_{\mathcal S'}\otimes_{\mathcal O_\mathcal S} \Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}.\]

Definition 5.6.

Let \(\mathcal S\) be a noetherian formal scheme and \(\mathcal X\) an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type. We define the ideal sheaf \(I_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) by \[I_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=I_\mathcal X^p(\Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S})\] and we set \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) the formal closed subscheme of \(\mathcal X\) defined by \(I_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\). Since \(\Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}=\varprojlim_n\Omega_{X_n|S_n}\), we have \[I_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\varprojlim_n I_{X_n}^p(X_n|S_n),\] \[J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\varprojlim_n J_{X_n}^p(X_n|S_n).\]

\(\mathcal X_{(x)}\) is an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type and we have a commutative diagram \[\xymatrix@C=4em@R=3em{\mathcal X_{(x)}\ar[r]^{i(\mathcal X)}\ar[dr]&\mathcal X\\&\mathcal X_{(x)}\ar[u]_{i(x)}}\] We note that if \(x\) is an isolated point of \(\mathcal X\), then \(i_{(x)},i(x)\) are both isomorphisms to the connected component of \(\mathcal X\) containing the point \(x\).

In particular, if \(|\mathcal X|=\{x_1,\ldots,x_m\}\) is a finite discrete space, then we have a canonical identification \[\mathcal X=\coprod_{1\leq i\leq m}\mathcal X_{(x_i)}\qquad\text{(disjoint union)}.\]

Corollary 5.7.

Let \(\mathcal S\) be a noetherian formal scheme, and \(\mathcal X\) an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type. We then have

  1. (1) \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) commutes with base change i.e., \[J_{\mathcal X'}^p(\mathcal X'|\mathcal S')=J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\times_\mathcal S\mathcal S'\] where \(\mathcal X'=\mathcal X\times_\mathcal S\mathcal S'\).
  2. (2) The connected components of \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) is in bijective correspondence with that of \(J_{X_0}^p(X_0|S_0)\). For each point \(x\in|J_{X_0}^p(X_0|S_0)|\) we have a canonical isomorphism \[J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)_{(x)}\simeq J_{\mathcal X_{(x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(x)}|\mathcal S).\] In particular if \[|J_{X_0}^p(X_0|S_0)|=\{x_1,\ldots,x_m\}\] is discrete, then \[J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\coprod_{1\leq i\leq m}J_{\mathcal X_{(x_i)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(x_i)}|\mathcal S)\] (disjoint union).
  3. (3) Let \(S\) be an ordinary noetherian scheme and \(X\) an \(S\)-scheme of essentially finite type. Given a closed subscheme \(S_0\subset S\), let \(\widehat S\) be the formal completion of \(S\) along \(S_0\). If we set \[\widehat X=X\times_S\widehat S=\text{the formal completion of }X\text{ along }X_0=X\times_SS_0,\] then \[J_{\widehat X}^p(\widehat X|\widehat S)=J_X^p(X|S)\times_S\widehat S =\text{the formal completion of }J_X^p(X|S)\text{ along }J_{X_0}^p(X_0|S_0).\]
  4. (4) If there exists a fixed integer \(n\geq0\) with the property 5.4(1) or (2) for \(X_\nu\to S_\nu\) for all \(\nu\), then \(x\notin J_\mathcal X^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) if and only if \(\mathcal X\) is formally smooth over \(\mathcal S\) at \(x\).

Proof: (1) follows from \(\Omega_{\mathcal X'|\mathcal S'}\simeq\mathcal O_{\mathcal S'}\widehat\otimes_{\mathcal O_\mathcal S} \Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}\) whereas (2) follows from \[\mathcal O_{\mathcal X_{(x)}}\widehat\otimes_{\mathcal O_\mathcal X} \Omega_{\mathcal X|\mathcal S}\simeq\Omega_{\mathcal X_{(x)}|\mathcal S}.\] (3) \[J_{\widehat X}^p(\widehat X|\widehat S)=\varprojlim_\nu J_{X\times_SS_\nu}^p(X\times_SS_\nu|S_\nu) =\varprojlim_\nu J_X^p(X|S)\times_SS_\nu=J_X^p(X|S)\times_S\widehat S.\] (4) Since \(\mathcal X\) is an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme, \[\mathcal X\to\mathcal S\text{ is formally smooth at }x \Longleftrightarrow X_\nu\to S_\nu\text{ is formally smooth at }x\text{ for all }\nu \Longleftrightarrow x\notin J_{X_\nu}^n(X_\nu|S_\nu)\text{ for all }\nu \Longleftrightarrow x\notin\varprojlim_\nu J_{X_\nu}^n(X_\nu|S_\nu)=J_\mathcal X^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S).\]

Remark 5.8.

Let \(\mathcal X\) be an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type. Given a point \(x\in\mathcal X\) we also consider the \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme \[\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}=\varprojlim_\nu\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_\nu,x}).\] We thus have the natural morphisms \(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}\to\mathcal X_{(x)}\to\mathcal X\). Now \(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x}\) is in general not essentially finite type over \(S_{(y)}\) where \(y\) is the image of \(x\) under \(\mathcal X\to\mathcal S\). For simplicity, let us set \(A_\nu=\mathcal O_{X_\nu,x}\), \(R_\nu=\mathcal O_{S_\nu,y}\). Now the natural map \(\Omega_{A_\nu|R_\nu}\to\widehat\Omega_{A_\nu|R_\nu}\) induces an isomorphism \[\widehat\Omega_{A_\nu|R_\nu}\to\widehat\Omega_{\widehat A_\nu|R_\nu},\] and therefore \[\varprojlim_\nu\widehat\Omega_{A_\nu|R_\nu}\simeq \varprojlim_\nu\widehat\Omega_{\widehat A_\nu|R_\nu},\] where \(\widehat{\ }\) stands for the separable completion of a module with respect to the topology induced from the ring. Therefore if we define \[\widehat\Omega_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S} =\varprojlim_\nu\widehat\Omega_{\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_\nu,x}|\mathcal O_S},\] then \(\widehat\Omega_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S}\) is a coherent \(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}\)-module. Thus if we set \(J_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S)\) to be the formal closed subscheme of \(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}\) defined by the ideal sheaf \[I_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S) =I_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}}^p(\widehat\Omega_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S}),\] then we have \[J_{\mathcal X_{(x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(x)}|\mathcal S)_{(\widehat x)} \simeq J_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S).\] We note that if \(x\) is an isolated point of \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\), then \[J_{\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x)}|\mathcal S)\to J_{\mathcal X_{(x)}}^p(\mathcal X_{(x)}|\mathcal S)\] is an isomorphism.

To consider the “image” of \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) under the morphism \(\mathcal X\xrightarrow f\mathcal S\), one may simply take the formal subscheme of \(\mathcal S\) defined by the ideal sheaves \(\operatorname{Ker}(\mathcal O_\mathcal S\to\mathcal O_\mathcal X/ I_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S))\). However, it is more convenient for our purpose to use the Fitting ideal again.

Definition 5.9.

Let \(\mathcal S\) be a noetherian formal scheme and \(f:\mathcal X\to\mathcal S\) an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme of essentially finite type. Assume that \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) is proper over \(\mathcal S\), so that \(f_*\mathcal O_{J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)}\) is a coherent \(\mathcal O_\mathcal S\)-module. We then define the ideal sheaf \(I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) of \(\mathcal O_\mathcal S\) by \[I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=I_\mathcal S^0 (f_*\mathcal O_{J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)}),\] and set \(J_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) to be the closed formal subscheme of \(\mathcal S\) defined by the ideal sheaf \(I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\). In case when there exists a fixed integer \(n\) having the property (1) or (2) in 5.4 for \(X_\nu\to S_\nu\) for all \(\nu\), we set \[I_\mathcal X(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=I_\mathcal X^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S),\qquad J_\mathcal X(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=J_\mathcal X^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S),\] \[I_\mathcal S(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=I_\mathcal S^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S),\qquad J_\mathcal S(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=J_\mathcal S^n(\mathcal X|\mathcal S).\]

Corollary 5.10.

Let \(\mathcal S\) be a noetherian formal scheme and \(\mathcal X\xrightarrow f\mathcal S\) an \(\mathcal S\)-adic formal scheme, essentially of finite type such that \(J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) is proper over \(\mathcal S\). Then

  2. (2) \(J_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\) commute with base change i.e., \[J_{\mathcal S'}^p(\mathcal X'|\mathcal S')=J_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)\times_\mathcal S\mathcal S'.\]
  3. (3) If \[J_\mathcal X^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\coprod_{1\leq i\leq m}\mathcal Y^{(i)}\quad\text{(disjoint union)},\] then \[I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_\mathcal S^0(f_*\mathcal O_{\mathcal Y^{(i)}}).\] In particular if \(|J_{X_0}^p(X_0|S_0)|=\{x_1,\ldots,x_m\}\) is discrete, then \[I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X|\mathcal S)=\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X_{(x_i)}|\mathcal S) =\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_\mathcal S^p(\mathcal X_{(\widehat x_i)}|\mathcal S).\]

If we set \(\widehat X=X\times_S\widehat S\) = the formal completion of \(X\) along \(X_0=X\times_SS_0\), then \[J_{\widehat S}^p(\widehat X|\widehat S)=J_S^p(X|S)\times_S\widehat S =\text{the formal completion of }J_S^p(X|S)\text{ along }J_{S_0}^p(X_0|S_0).\]

Remark 5.11.

Our definition of Jacobian ideal, adopted from the classical one over base field, commutes with base change and gives a smoothness criteria under a mild condition of 5.4. However one can define, probably in many different ways, a canonical subscheme which gives the non-smooth locus in a general setting. We shall outline one here: Given an \(A\)-algebra \(B\), define \[I_{B|A}=\bigcap_E\operatorname{Ann}D^1(B|A,E)\] where \(E\) runs through all \(B\)-modules of finite type. This ideal \(I_{B|A}\) commutes with localizations by 3.13(c), and therefore, given a morphism \(X\xrightarrow fY\), we obtain the ideal sheaf \(I_{X|Y}\). If \(f\) is a morphism of essentially finite type, then \(f\) is smooth at \(x\) if and only if \(x\notin\operatorname{Supp}\mathcal O_X/I_{X|Y}\) by 3.13(a). This notion is in an obvious way carried over to adic formal schemes of essentially finite type and thus it is equally suitable for deformation theory.

6. Non-degenerate quadratic singularities

The objective in this section is to investigate the deformations of a \(k\)-scheme having non-degenerate quadratic singularities only. In view of 4.13, it becomes purely a local problem in an appropriate situation.

We go back to the notations of §4. Thus \(\Lambda\) is a fixed complete noetherian local ring with the residue field \(k\), and \(C_\Lambda\) stands for the category of local artinian \(\Lambda\)-algebras with the same residue field \(k\).

Let \(X\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type, and let \(x\) be a closed point of \(X\). We recall that the point \(x\) is said to be a non-degenerate quadratic singularity rational over \(k\) if \[\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X,x}=k[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(f)\quad\text{with}\quad (\partial f/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n)=(x_1,\ldots,x_n).\] One can always assume \(f\) to be a quadratic form. Indeed we have

Lemma 6.1.

Let \(f\in k[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]\). If \((\partial f/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n)=M\;(=(x_1,\ldots,x_n))\), then one can find \(x'_1,\ldots,x'_n\) with \(x_i=x'_i\pmod {M^2}\) such that \[f=q(x'_1,\ldots,x'_n)\] where \(q\) is a quadratic form.

Proof: We do this by a successive approximation. Write \(f=f_2+h_3\) where \(f_2\) is a quadratic form and \(h_3\in M^3\). Since \((\partial f/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n)=M\), there exists \(\epsilon_1^{(1)},\ldots,\epsilon_n^{(1)}\) in \(M^2\) such that \[\sum\epsilon_i^{(1)}\partial f/\partial x_i=h_3\pmod {M^4}.\] If we set \(x_i^{(1)}=x_i-\epsilon_i^{(1)}\), then \[f(x_1^{(1)},\ldots,x_n^{(1)})= f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)-\sum\epsilon_i^{(1)}\partial f/\partial x_i+\cdots =f_2(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\pmod {M^4},\] i.e., \[f(x_1^{(1)},\ldots,x_n^{(1)})=f_2(x_1,\ldots,x_n)+h_4\quad\text{with }h_4\in M^4.\] Now \[(\partial f/\partial x_1^{(1)},\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n^{(1)}) =(\partial f/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n)=M\] and therefore we can choose

\(\epsilon_1^{(2)},\ldots,\epsilon_n^{(2)}\) in \(M^3\) such that \[\sum\epsilon_i^{(2)}\partial f/\partial x_i^{(1)}=h_4\pmod {M^5}.\] Then \[f(x_1^{(1)}-\epsilon_1^{(2)},\ldots,x_n^{(1)}-\epsilon_n^{(2)}) =f(x_1^{(1)},\ldots,x_n^{(1)})-\sum\epsilon_i^{(2)}\partial f/\partial x_i^{(1)}+\cdots =f_2(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\pmod {M^5}.\] Continuing this process we can find \(\epsilon_i^{(1)},\epsilon_i^{(2)},\epsilon_i^{(3)},\ldots\) (\(i=1,2,\ldots,n\)) with \(\epsilon_i^{(v)}\in M^{v+1}\) such that if we set \[x_i^{(v)}=x_i-(\epsilon_i^{(1)}+\cdots+\epsilon_i^{(v)}),\] then \(f(x_1^{(v)},\ldots,x_n^{(v)})=f_2(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\pmod {M^{v+3}}\). Thus if we set \(\epsilon_i=\sum_v\epsilon_i^{(v)}\), then \[f(x_1-\epsilon_1,\ldots,x_n-\epsilon_n)=f_2(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\] i.e., \(f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)=f_2(x_1+\epsilon_1,\ldots,x_n+\epsilon_n)\).

Lemma 6.2.

Let \(A_0\) be a local \(k\)-algebra, essentially of finite type such that \[\widehat A_0\simeq k[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(f)\] where \(f\) is a non-degenerate quadratic form, and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0=\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)\). Then

  1. (1) \(J_{\mathcal X}(\mathcal X|R)\to J_R(\mathcal X|R)\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) are both isomorphisms.
  2. (2) \(I_R(\mathcal X|R)\) is a principal ideal in \(R\) generated by a free formal generator of \(R\) over \(\Lambda\), i.e. \(I_R(\mathcal X|R)=(t)\) and \(R=\Lambda[[t]]\).
  3. (3) \(\mathcal X\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth.

Proof: We set \(\mathcal X=\operatorname{Spf}(A)\). By 4.10(b), \((R,\operatorname{Spf}(\widehat A))\) is a formal versal deformation of \(\widehat X_0=\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat A_0)\), where \[\widehat A_0=\varprojlim_v A\otimes_R R_v.\] Now \(\widehat A_0\simeq k[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(f)\) and \((\partial f/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial f/\partial x_n)=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) in \(k[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]\). It follows readily that \([M_{\widehat X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\simeq k\) and, by 4.14, we get \[R\simeq\Lambda[[t]]\quad\text{and}\quad \widehat A\simeq R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-t)\] where \(F\) is a quadratic polynomial in \(\Lambda[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\) (\(\subset R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]\)) obtained from \(f\) by lifting the coefficients to \(\Lambda\). Now \[0\to\widehat A\xrightarrow{dF}\widehat A dx_1\oplus\cdots\oplus\widehat A dx_n \longrightarrow\widehat\Omega_{\widehat A|R}\to0\] is exact where \(dF\colon\widehat A\to\widehat A dx_1\oplus\cdots\oplus\widehat A dx_n\) is given by \[1\longmapsto(\partial F/\partial x_1)dx_1+\cdots+(\partial F/\partial x_n)dx_n,\] and therefore \[I_{\widehat A}(\widehat A|R)=(\partial F/\partial x_1,\ldots,\partial F/\partial x_n) =(x_1,\ldots,x_n),\] and hence \[\widehat A/I_{\widehat A}(\widehat A|R)=\widehat A/(x_1,\ldots,x_n) =R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(x_1,\ldots,x_n,F-t)\simeq\Lambda.\] Thus the canonical map \(\Lambda\to\widehat A/I_{\widehat A}(\widehat A|R)\) is an isomorphism. It follows that \(\widehat A\to\widehat A/I_{\widehat A}(\widehat A|R)\) is an isomorphism, and consequently \(\Lambda\to R/I_R(\mathcal X|R)\) is also an isomorphism. This proves (1) and (2). On the other hand, (3) follows from \[\widehat A\simeq R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-t) \simeq\Lambda[[t,x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-t)\simeq\Lambda[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]\] as \(\Lambda\)-algebras.

Corollary 6.3.

Let \(X_0\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type, and let \(x\) in \(X_0\) be a non-degenerate quadratic singularity rational over \(k\).

  1. (1) Let \((R,\mathcal X)\), \((R,\mathcal Y)\) be any two deformations of \(X_0\) where \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(\widehat C_\Lambda)\). Assume that \(k=k^2\). Then \(\mathcal X_{(x)}\simeq\mathcal Y_{(x)}\) as \(R\)-adic formal schemes if and only if \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)=I_R(\mathcal Y_{(x)}|R)\).
  2. (2) Let \(R\in\operatorname{ob}(\widehat C_\Lambda)\) be a regular local ring, and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a deformation of \(X_0\). Then \(\mathcal O_{\mathcal X,x}\) is a regular local ring if and only if \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)\not\subset\mathfrak m_R^2\), i.e., if and only if \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)\) is a regular divisor in \(\operatorname{Spf}(R)\).

Proof: Let \((R_0,\mathcal Z)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x})\). Since \(x\) in \(X_0\) is a non-degenerate quadratic singularity rational over \(k\), we may assume that \[R_0\simeq\Lambda[[t]]\quad\text{and}\quad \mathcal Z\simeq\operatorname{Spf}(R_0[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-t))\] as in 6.2.

(1) \((R,\mathcal X_{(x)})\), \((R,\mathcal Y_{(x)})\) are deformations of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,x})\), and therefore they are induced from \((R_0,\mathcal Z)\) by continuous local \(\Lambda\)-algebra maps \(\varphi,\psi\colon R_0\to R\) respectively, i.e., if we set \(a=\varphi(t)\), \(b=\psi(t)\), then \[\mathcal X_{(x)}\simeq\operatorname{Spf}(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-a)),\qquad \mathcal Y_{(x)}\simeq\operatorname{Spf}(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-b))\] as \(R\)-adic formal schemes. Assume that \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)=I_R(\mathcal Y_{(x)}|R)\), i.e., \((a)=(b)\) in \(R\). Then \(a=ub\) for some unit \(u\) in \(R\). Since \(k=k^2\) and \(R\) is complete, we can write \(u=v^2\) for some unit \(v\) in \(R\), i.e., \(a=v^2b\). Then \((F-b)=(v^2F-a)\), so that \(X_i\mapsto vX_i\) establishes an \(R\)-algebra isomorphism \[R[[X_1,\ldots,X_n]]/(F-a)\xrightarrow{\sim}R[[X_1,\ldots,X_n]]/(F-b)\] since \(F\) is a quadratic form. The other direction is trivial.

(2) There exists a continuous local \(\Lambda\)-algebra map \(\varphi\colon R_0=\Lambda[[t]]\to R\) such that \[\mathcal X_{(x)}\simeq\operatorname{Spf}(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-\varphi(t))).\] We note that \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)=(\varphi(t))\), and that \(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-\varphi(t))=\widehat{\mathcal O}_{\mathcal X,x}\) is regular if and only if \(\mathcal O_{\mathcal X,x}\) is regular. Now \(R\) is regular and hence \(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]\) is also a regular local ring. Thus if \(M\) is its maximal ideal, then \(R[[x_1,\ldots,x_n]]/(F-\varphi(t))\) is regular if and only if \(F-\varphi(t)\notin M^2\), i.e. if and only if \(\varphi(t)\notin\mathfrak m_R^2\), i.e. if and only if \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(x)}|R)\not\subset\mathfrak m_R^2\).

Theorem 6.4.

Let \(X_0\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type with isolated non-smooth points only, and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\). Denote by \(\{z_1,\ldots,z_m\}\) the set of non-smooth points in \(X_0\). Assume that \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\), and all \(z_i\)'s are non-degenerate quadratic singularity rational over \(k\). Then

  1. (1) \(R\) is formally smooth over \(\Lambda\), i.e., \(R\) is a formal power-series ring over \(\Lambda\);
  2. (2) \(J_{\mathcal X}(\mathcal X|R)=\coprod_{1\leq i\leq m}J_{\mathcal X_{(z_i)}}(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\) (disjoint union), and \(J_{\mathcal X_{(z_i)}}(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\xrightarrow{\sim}J_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\) is an isomorphism;
  3. (3) \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\) is a principal ideal and in turn so is \[I_R(\mathcal X|R)=\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R).\] Furthermore if we set \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)=(t_i)\), then \(t_1,\ldots,t_m\) can be extended to a system of free formal generators of \(R\) over \(\Lambda\) (as formal power-series ring over \(\Lambda\)).
  4. (4) \(\mathcal X\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is smooth, i.e. \(J_{\mathcal X}(\mathcal X|\Lambda)=\varnothing\).
  5. (5) If \(X_0\) is a complete irreducible curve, then \[\dim M_{X_0}=3g-3+a\] where \(g=\dim_k H^1(X_0,\mathcal O_{X_0})\) and \(a=\dim_k H^0(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\).

Proof: (1) follows from (2) and (3). The first statement of (2) follows from 5.7(2). Now let \((R_i,\mathcal Y_i)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,z_i})\). Since \((R,\mathcal X_{(z_i)})\) is a deformation of \(\operatorname{Spec}(\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X_0,z_i})\), there exists a continuous local \(\Lambda\)-algebra map \(\varphi_i\colon R_i\to R\) with a cartesian diagram \[\begin{array}{ccc} \mathcal X_{(z_i)}&\longrightarrow&\mathcal Y_i\\ \downarrow&&\downarrow\\ \operatorname{Spf}(R)&\xrightarrow{\operatorname{Spf}(\varphi_i)}&\operatorname{Spf}(R_i). \end{array}\] Therefore we have \[J_{\mathcal X_{(z_i)}}(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R) =J_{\mathcal X_{(z_i)}}(\mathcal Y_i|R_i)\times_{\operatorname{Spf}(R_i)}\operatorname{Spf}(R),\] \[J_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R) =J_{R_i}(\mathcal Y_i|R_i)\times_{\operatorname{Spf}(R_i)}\operatorname{Spf}(R).\] Since \(J_{\mathcal Y_i}(\mathcal Y_i|R_i)\to J_{R_i}(\mathcal Y_i|R_i)\) is an isomorphism by 6.2(1), the canonical morphism \(J_{\mathcal X_{(z_i)}}(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\to J_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\) is an isomorphism. Now \(I_{R_i}(\mathcal Y_i|R_i)=(u_i)=(u_i)\), with \(R_i=\Lambda[[u_i]]\) by 6.2(2), and therefore \[I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)=I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)=(t_i)\] where \(t_i=\varphi_i(u_i)\), and therefore \[I_R(\mathcal X|R)=\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R) =\left(\prod_{i=1}^m t_i\right).\] Now \(M_{X_0}\to M_{X_0,(z_1)}\times\cdots\times M_{X_0,(z_m)}\) is smooth and therefore \[R_1\widehat\otimes_\Lambda\cdots\widehat\otimes_\Lambda R_m\to R\] is formally smooth by 4.13, which implies that \(t_1,\ldots,t_m\) is extended to a system of free formal generators of \(R\) over \(\Lambda\).

(4) The projection morphism \(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}\simeq\mathcal Y_i\times_{\operatorname{Spf}(R_i)}\operatorname{Spf}(R) \to\mathcal Y_i\) is formally smooth since \(\operatorname{Spf}(R)\xrightarrow{\operatorname{Spf}(\varphi_i)}\operatorname{Spf}(R_i)\) is formally smooth. On the other hand, \(\mathcal Y_i\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth by 6.2(3) and therefore \(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth. Consequently \(\mathcal X\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth since \(\{z_1,\ldots,z_m\}\) is the set of non-smooth points of \(X_0\).

(5) Since \(R\) is formally smooth over \(\Lambda\) and \[0\to H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\to[M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])] \to H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0})\to0\] is exact by 4.4 and 4.11, we have \[\dim M_{X_0}=\dim k\otimes R =\dim_k H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})+\dim H^0(X_0,D^1_{X_0}) =\dim_k H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})+m.\] Then it suffices to show that \[\dim_k H^1(X_0,D^0_{X_0})+m=3g-3+\dim_k H^0(X_0,D^0_{X_0}),\] i.e., \(\chi(D^0_{X_0})=3-3g+m\), where we set \(\chi(E)=\dim_k H^0(X_0,E)-\dim_k H^1(X_0,E)\) for any coherent \(X_0\)-module \(E\). Since \(\chi(D^0_{X_0})\) does not change when one replaces the base field \(k\) by its algebraic closure, we may assume that \(k\) is algebraically closed. Set \(A=\mathcal O_{X_0,z}\), where \(z\) is a non-smooth point of \(X_0\), and let \(A'\) be its normalization. By hypothesis, we have \(A\simeq k[[x,y]]/(f)\) where \(f\) is a non-degenerate quadratic form. Since \(k\) is algebraically closed, we may assume that \(f=xy\), i.e., \(A\simeq k[[x,y]]/(xy)\). From this we conclude immediately that \(\ell(A'/A)=1\) and \(\operatorname{Der}_k(A,\mathfrak m_A)=\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\). Let \(C\) be the conductor of \(A\) in \(A'\). Since \(A\) is a complete-intersection we must have that \(\ell(A/C)=\ell(A'/A)=1\), so that \(C=\mathfrak m_A\). We thus obtain that \[A'=\{a'\in K\mid a'\mathfrak m_A\subset\mathfrak m_A\}\] where \(K\) is the fraction field of \(A\). We now claim that \(\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\subset\operatorname{Der}_k(A',A')\) as \(A\)-modules contained in \(\operatorname{Der}_k(K,K)\). Indeed, let \(X\in\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\). Then for any \(a'\in A'\) and \(x\in\mathfrak m_A\) we have \(xa'\in\mathfrak m_A\) so that \(X(xa')\in\mathfrak m_A\) and \(a'X(x)\in\mathfrak m_A\) since \(\operatorname{Der}_k(A,\mathfrak m_A)=\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\). Consequently \(X(xa')=a'X(x)+xX(a')\) entails that \(xX(a')\in\mathfrak m_A\) for all \(x\in\mathfrak m_A\), and therefore \(X(a')\in A'\). Furthermore, the exact sequence \[0\to\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\to\operatorname{Der}_k(A',A') \to A'/\mathfrak m_AA'\to0\] entails the exact sequence \[0\to\operatorname{Der}_k(A,A)\to\operatorname{Der}_k(A',A')\to A'/C\to0.\] Therefore if we set \(X_0'\) to be the normalization of \(X_0\), then \[0\to D^0_{X_0}\to D^0_{X_0'}\to\mathcal O_{X_0'}/C\to0\] is exact. Since \(X_0\) is locally a complete-intersection, we have \(\ell(\mathcal O_{X_0'}/C)=2\ell(\mathcal O_{X_0'}/\mathcal O_{X_0})=2m\), whereas \(\chi(D^0_{X_0'})=3-3(g-m)\) by Riemann-Roch theorem. Consequently \[\chi(D^0_{X_0})=\chi(D^0_{X_0'})-\ell(\mathcal O_{X_0'}/C) =3-3(g-m)-2m=3-3g+m.\] This completes our proof.

Remark 6.5. In the formula 6.4(5), the number \(a=H^0(X_0,D^0_{X_0})\) can be easily computed in terms of Riemann-Roch theorem. Thus \[a=\begin{cases} 0&\text{if }g\geq2,\\ 1&\text{if }g=1,\\ 3&\text{if }g=0. \end{cases}\] Finally we add a remark on characteristic 2. If the characteristic of the base field \(k\) is 2, there is no non-degenerate quadratic form on \(n\) variables if \(n\) is odd. In other words there does not exist a non-degenerate quadratic singularity of even dimension if \(\operatorname{char}k=2\). In this case one may consider a quadratic form of maximum non-degeneracy:

Definition 6.6.

Let \(X\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type. A closed point \(z\in X\) is called an ordinary quadratic singularity rational over \(k\) if the following holds:

  1. (i) If either \(\operatorname{char}k\ne2\) or \(\operatorname{char}k=2\) and \(\dim\mathcal O_{X,z}\) is odd, then \(z\) is a non-degenerate quadratic singularity rational over \(k\).
  2. (ii) If \(\operatorname{char}k=2\) and \(\dim\mathcal O_{X,z}\) is even, then \[\bar{k}\widehat\otimes_k\widehat{\mathcal O}_{X,z} \simeq\bar{k}[[x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}]]/(f)\] with \[f=x_0^2+x_1x_2+x_3x_4+\cdots+x_{2n-1}x_{2n}\] where \(\bar{k}\) is the algebraic closure of \(k\).
Lemma 6.7.

Let \(k\) be a field of characteristic 2 and set \[A_0=k[[x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}]]/(f)\] where \[f=x_0^2+x_1x_2+x_3x_4+\cdots+x_{2n-1}x_{2n},\] and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0=\operatorname{Spec}(A_0)\). Then \(R=\Lambda[[t,t_1]]\), where \(t,t_1\) are free formal generators of \(R\) over \(\Lambda\), such that \(I_R(\mathcal X|R)=(t^2)\), and \(\mathcal X\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth.

Proof: We set \(\mathcal X=\operatorname{Spf}(A)\). Since \[\left(f,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_0},\ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_{2n}}\right)=(x_0^2,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n})\] it follows that \(D^1(A_0|k,A_0)\simeq k[x_0]/(x_0^2)\) and therefore \([M_{X_0}(k[\epsilon])]\) is 2-dimensional vector space over \(k\). Furthermore, it follows from 4.14 that \[R=\Lambda[[t,t_1]]\quad\text{and}\quad A\simeq R[[x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}]]/(F)\] where \(F=f+t_1+tx_0\). Now the exact sequence \[0\to A\xrightarrow{dF}A\,dx_0\oplus\cdots\oplus A\,dx_{2n} \to\widehat\Omega_{A|R}\to0\] where \[dF\colon1\longmapsto \sum_{0\leq i\leq2n}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\,dx_i+t\,dx_0,\] entails that \[I_A(A|R)=(t,x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_{2n})=(x_0^2+t_1,t,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n})\] so that \[A/I_A(A|R)\simeq A/(x_0^2+t_1,t,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}) \simeq\Lambda[[t_1,x_0]]/(x_0^2-t_1).\] Since \(\Lambda[[t_1,x_0]]/(x_0^2-t_1)\simeq R/(t)\oplus R/(t)\) as \(R\)-modules, it follows that \(I_R(A|R)=(t^2)\), which proves our first assertion. On the other hand, \[A\simeq\Lambda[[t,t_1,x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}]]/ (x_0^2+x_1x_2+\cdots+x_{2n-1}x_{2n}+tx_0+t_1) \simeq\Lambda[[t,x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{2n}]]\] and therefore \(A\) is formally smooth over \(\Lambda\).

The above statement 6.7 yields a similar result as 6.4, proof of which we leave to the readers.

Corollary 6.8.

Let \(X_0\) be a \(k\)-scheme of finite type with isolated non-smooth points only such that \(H^2(X_0,D^0_{X_0})=0\), and let \((R,\mathcal X)\) be a formal versal deformation of \(X_0\). Denote by \(\{z_1,\ldots,z_m\}\) the set of non-smooth points in \(X_0\) and assume that \(\operatorname{char}k=2\), \(\dim X_0\) is even, and all \(z_i\)'s are ordinary quadratic singularities. Then

  1. (1) \(R\) is formally smooth over \(\Lambda\).
  2. (2) \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)\) is a principal ideal and in turn so is \[I_R(\mathcal X|R)=\prod_{1\leq i\leq m}I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R).\] Furthermore, \(I_R(\mathcal X_{(z_i)}|R)=(t_i^2)\) and \(t_1,\ldots,t_m\) can be extended to a system of free formal generators of \(R\) over \(\Lambda\).
  3. (3) \(\mathcal X\to\operatorname{Spec}(\Lambda)\) is formally smooth.
